How to Cite Sources Properly

Proper citation builds credibility with our skeptical audience. Always attribute statistics clearly and be prepared to provide source documentation when asked.

Citation Formats by Context

Website / Marketing Copy

Use inline attribution with abbreviated source names. Link to full source when possible.

According to Harvard Business Review, 67% of consulting projects fail to meet stated objectives.*

*Source: Harvard Business Review, "When Consulting Fails", 2023

Proposals / Formal Documents

Use footnote-style citations with full source information.

Research indicates that 89% of business owners want to see projected ROI before signing an engagement agreement.[1]

[1] Smith, J. & Johnson, M. (2024). "Small Business Decision-Making Report." B2B Research Institute. Retrieved from [URL].

Sales Conversations

Reference sources naturally in conversation, be prepared to share documentation.

"A Harvard study found that about two-thirds of consulting engagements don't meet their objectives. That's why we do things differently - I can send you that study if you'd like to review it."

Internal ScaleUp Data

Always specify the methodology and time period for internal statistics.

Source: ScaleUp Ventures Internal Client Tracking, Analysis of 47 engagements from January 2020 - December 2024. Methodology available upon request.

Source Categories and Requirements

Source Type Required Information Verification Level
Academic/Research Author, Title, Publication, Year, Page/Section Keep PDF copy on file
Industry Reports Organization, Report Title, Year, Page Number Keep PDF or subscription access
Internal Data Metric, Time Period, Sample Size, Methodology Full data set available internally
Client Testimonials Name, Title, Company, Date, Written Permission Signed release on file
News/Press Publication, Article Title, Author, Date, URL Screenshot + URL archived

Citation Guidelines for Our Audience

  • Be conservative: Our audience is skeptical. Don't overstate or cherry-pick data.
  • Prefer recent sources: Use data from the last 3 years when possible.
  • Be prepared to defend: If you cite it, be ready to provide the full source.
  • Distinguish estimates: Make clear when a figure is an estimate vs. measured data.
  • Update regularly: Remove or update outdated statistics (see Update Schedule).

Data Visualization Recommendations

Use these visualization guidelines to present proof points effectively. Our skeptical audience values clarity and substance over flashy graphics.

Bar Charts
Best for comparing our performance against industry benchmarks. Use when showing ScaleUp vs. industry averages.
Use for: Comparison data, before/after metrics, benchmark data
Line Charts
Best for showing progress over time. Use when displaying ROI trajectory or client outcome trends.
Use for: Time-series data, ROI growth, progress tracking
Donut/Pie Charts
Best for showing proportions. Use sparingly - only when parts of a whole are relevant.
Use for: Market share, success rate breakdowns, budget allocation
Data Tables
Best for detailed comparisons. Use when audience needs to see exact figures and multiple variables.
Use for: Detailed ROI breakdowns, metric comparisons, case study data
Single Stat Callouts
Best for hero sections and key messages. One powerful number with context is often more effective than a complex chart.
Use for: Headlines, key differentiators, impact statements
Projected vs. Actual
Signature ScaleUp visualization. Show projected outcomes alongside actual results to demonstrate accuracy.
Use for: Case studies, proposal projections, client results

Brand-Aligned Visualization Guidelines

Element Specification Rationale
Primary Color Navy #0F1729 Authority, trust, professionalism
Accent Color Gold #D4A853 Highlight key data, calls to action
Positive Metrics #059669 (Green) Growth, success, achievement
Negative Metrics #DC2626 (Red) Industry problems, failures to avoid
Typography Clean sans-serif, 14px minimum Readability for 30-55 audience
Labels Always include, never assume Clarity over elegance
Sources Always visible, not hidden Build trust through transparency

Visualization Dos and Don'ts

Do:

  • Include source attribution on every chart
  • Use large, readable fonts (14px+ for our audience)
  • Lead with the insight, not the visualization
  • Show projected vs. actual whenever possible

Don't:

  • Use 3D effects, animations, or decorative elements
  • Truncate axes to exaggerate differences
  • Present data without context or comparison
  • Use more than 5 colors in a single visualization
