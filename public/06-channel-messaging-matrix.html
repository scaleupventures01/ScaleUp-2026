Channel-Specific Messaging Matrix

ScaleUp Ventures Brand Communication Guide

"Certainty First."

How to Use This Matrix

This comprehensive guide ensures consistent, on-brand messaging across all ScaleUp Ventures communication channels. Each channel has specific tone adjustments, message lengths, key phrases, and visual requirements calibrated for our target audience: skeptical 30-55 year old business owners who value substance over flash and evidence over promises.

Watchful: Observant, thorough, nothing escapes notice
Measured: Deliberate, precise, no wasted words
Decisive: Clear direction, confident recommendations

Example Homepage Hero Copy

"Your business decisions shouldn't feel like gambles. ScaleUp Ventures brings clarity to growth strategy, helping established business owners navigate expansion with confidence and precision. Certainty first."

Visual Requirements - Website

Color Ratio 70% white/off-white, 20% navy, 10% gold accents
Typography Crimson Pro for H1-H3, DM Sans for body and UI
Imagery Professional, mature subjects. No stock-photo smiles.
White Space Generous margins. Never crowded.

Do

  • Lead with outcomes and results
  • Use specific numbers when available
  • Address skepticism directly and honestly
  • Keep sentences under 20 words
  • Use active voice consistently
  • Include clear navigation and logical flow

Don't

  • Use buzzwords like "synergy" or "disruption"
  • Make claims without supporting evidence
  • Use exclamation points
  • Include stock photos of handshakes
  • Bury the value proposition
  • Use sliders or auto-playing elements

2 Email Communications

Email Type Tone Adjustment Length Guidelines Key Messages CTA Approach
Cold Outreach Respectful + Relevant Brief (75-125 words) Reference specific trigger (event, news, mutual connection). One clear value proposition. Acknowledge their time is valuable. Low-commitment (15-min call)
Nurture Sequence Educational + Patient Moderate (150-250 words) Provide genuine value without selling. Share relevant insights. Build credibility through expertise. Soft (read more, download guide)
Proposals Precise + Confident Comprehensive (as needed) Restate the problem clearly. Present methodology transparently. Detail deliverables, timeline, investment. Direct (Let's proceed)
Follow-up Persistent + Graceful Brief (50-100 words) Add new value with each touch. Never guilt or pressure. Offer easy next step. Single, clear ask

Character Limits

  • Subject lines: 40-60 characters
  • Preview text: 80-100 characters
  • Cold email body: Under 150 words
  • Paragraphs: 2-3 sentences max

Subject Line Templates

Type Template Example
Cold Outreach [Specific observation] + [Question/value] "Question about your Q2 expansion"
Nurture [Insight] + [for/about] + [their situation] "3 signals that indicate growth readiness"
Follow-up [Direct reference] + [new element] "Following up + relevant case study"
Proposal [Company name] + [Engagement type] "ScaleUp Ventures - Growth Strategy Proposal"
Example Cold Outreach Email

Subject: Question about your regional expansion

[Name],

I noticed your recent announcement about expanding into the Midwest market. That's a significant move for a 25-year-old business.

Having guided similar manufacturers through regional expansion, I've observed that timing and sequence often matter more than capital. The businesses that struggle usually had the resources but moved on the wrong signals.

If you're still mapping your approach, I'd value 15 minutes to share what I've seen work. No pitch, just perspective.

Worth a conversation?

[Signature]

Do

  • Personalize beyond just the name
  • Front-load the value
  • Use simple, direct language
  • Include one clear CTA per email
  • Respect the 48-hour follow-up gap
  • Sign with full name and title

Don't

  • Use "Just checking in" as an opener
  • Send more than 3 follow-ups
  • Use calendar scheduling links in cold emails
  • Include multiple asks or CTAs
  • Write paragraphs longer than 3 lines
  • Use "Hope this finds you well"

Example LinkedIn Post

Most business owners I meet are solving the wrong problem.

They say: "I need to grow revenue."

But when we examine the situation carefully, the real constraint is often capacity, not demand. They're already at 85% utilization. Adding revenue without addressing that creates chaos, not growth.

The question isn't "how do we sell more?"

It's "what must be true before we can handle more?"

Sequence matters. Growth without readiness is just expensive learning.

What's the constraint your business is actually facing right now?

Do

  • Use line breaks for readability
  • Start with a compelling first line (the hook)
  • Share genuine insights from experience
  • Engage thoughtfully on others' posts first
  • Post consistently (2-3x per week)
  • Respond to every comment within 24 hours

Don't

  • Use hashtags excessively (max 3-5)
  • Post motivational quotes without substance
  • Pitch in InMail without building rapport
  • Use engagement bait ("Comment YES if...")
  • Tag people who haven't consented
  • Share only company content

4 Proposals and Sales Decks

Section Tone Content Guidelines Length
Executive Summary Confident + Concise Problem statement, proposed solution, expected outcomes, investment summary. Decision-maker should get full picture from this page alone. 1 page max
Situation Analysis Precise + Observant Demonstrate understanding of their specific situation. Reference discovery conversations. Show you've done the work. 1-2 pages
Recommended Approach Authoritative + Clear Phase-by-phase methodology. Specific deliverables per phase. Timeline with milestones. Rationale for approach. 2-3 pages
Investment Direct + Transparent Clear pricing structure. What's included/excluded. Payment terms. Value justification (ROI framing). 1 page
Credentials Relevant + Restrained Case studies similar to their situation. Specific outcomes achieved. Relevant experience only. 1-2 pages
Next Steps Decisive + Simple Clear path to engagement. What happens when they say yes. Timeline to start. Contact information. Half page

Visual Requirements - Proposals and Decks

Layout Clean, ample white space. One idea per slide.
Color Usage Navy for headers, Gold for accents and key data
Typography Min 14pt body, 24pt+ headers. Never justify text.
Charts Simple, labeled clearly. No 3D effects.
Branding Logo on cover and footer. Consistent throughout.
Page Count Proposals: 8-12 pages. Decks: 10-15 slides.
Example Executive Summary Opening

"Meridian Manufacturing has built a $12M regional business through operational excellence and customer loyalty. The question now is how to replicate that success in two new markets without overextending the core business.

Based on our discovery conversations and analysis, we recommend a phased approach that sequences market entry to match capacity development. This protects the foundation while building new revenue streams."

Do

  • Customize every proposal (no templates)
  • Lead with their situation, not your services
  • Include specific deliverables with timelines
  • Make pricing clear and defensible
  • Provide a printed and digital version
  • Follow up with a brief summary email

Don't

  • Lead with company history or credentials
  • Use jargon or consultant-speak
  • Include testimonials from unrelated industries
  • Bury the investment section
  • Send without a scheduled review call
  • Use complex animations or transitions

Growth Strategy for Established Businesses

For business owners who've built something worth protecting, expansion requires more than ambition. It requires a clear-eyed view of capacity, timing, and sequence.

Do

  • Invest in premium paper and printing
  • Include QR codes linking to relevant pages
  • Use generous margins and white space
  • Ensure readability (adequate font size)
  • Keep content evergreen (avoid dates)
  • Proof carefully - print errors are permanent

Don't

  • Print on standard office paper
  • Include dated pricing or offers
  • Use glossy finishes (too flashy for brand)
  • Crowd the layout with too much text
  • Include every service offering
  • Use stock photos of generic business scenes

Quick Reference: Channel Summary

Website

Confident + Welcoming. Lead with outcomes. Clear navigation. Generous white space. CTA: Schedule consultation.

Email

Respectful + Relevant. Under 150 words for cold. Personalize beyond name. One CTA per email. No "checking in."

LinkedIn

Insightful + Conversational. 800-1200 char posts. Share real observations. Engage on others' content first. 3-4x weekly.

Proposals

Precise + Confident. Lead with their situation. Specific deliverables. Clear investment section. Always customize.

Print

Premium + Understated. Heavy paper stock. Generous margins. QR codes to digital. No dated content.

Advertising

Direct + Targeted. Problem-aware headlines. Value-first offers. Precise targeting. Dedicated landing pages.

+ Universal Guidelines

Core Voice Across All Channels

Voice Attribute What It Means What It Doesn't Mean Consistent Across Channels
Watchful Observant, thorough, sees what others miss Paranoid, suspicious, negative Always demonstrate that we've noticed the details others overlook
Measured Deliberate, precise, no wasted words Slow, hesitant, indecisive Every word earns its place. Cut unnecessary qualifiers.
Decisive Clear direction, confident recommendations Pushy, arrogant, inflexible Take clear positions. Avoid hedge words like "maybe" or "perhaps."

Universal Phrases to Use

Certainty First Evidence-based Clear-eyed view Worth protecting Sequence matters Built on experience Capacity before demand The right signals Prepared, not just ambitious Sustainable growth

Universal Phrases to Avoid

Synergy Disruptive Game-changing Leverage Best-in-class Cutting-edge Revolutionary Just checking in Hope this helps Circle back

Always (Every Channel)

  • Lead with the audience's concern, not our capabilities
  • Use specific language over generic claims
  • Back assertions with evidence when possible
  • Write at a 10th-grade reading level
  • Use active voice and strong verbs
  • Proofread everything twice
  • Include a clear next step

Never (Every Channel)

  • Use exclamation points
  • Make unsubstantiated claims
  • Use jargon our audience wouldn't use
  • Talk about ourselves more than them
  • Pressure or create false urgency
  • Publish without brand review for new assets
  • Forget the fundamental: Certainty First
