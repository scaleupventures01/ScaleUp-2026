On being burned by agencies
I spent $47,000 on a marketing agency that promised me the world. Six months later, I had a pretty website and exactly zero new clients. When I asked about results, they sent me a 40-page report full of "impressions" and "reach" and "engagement rates." Metrics that meant nothing to my bottom line. I learned something expensive that day: Most agencies are in the business of selling activity, not outcomes. They bill hours. They deliver "work." They send invoices. What they don't do is show you - before you spend a dollar - exactly how you'll make that money back. After 25 years in business, I've talked to hundreds of owners with the same story. About half of us have been burned by an agency that overpromised and underdelivered. Here's what I wish someone had told me earlier: Before you hire anyone, ask one simple question: "Can you show me the math on how I make my money back?" If they can't - or won't - that tells you everything you need to know. The right partner proves the value before asking for your investment. Questions to ask consultants
Before you hire your next consultant, ask these 5 questions: 1. "Can you show me - before I pay you anything - how I'll make my investment back?" If they say "it depends" or "results vary," that's a red flag. Good consultants can build a model with your actual numbers. 2. "Who owns the work product at the end?" Some agencies hold your website, your data, or your accounts hostage. Get this in writing upfront. 3. "What happens if this doesn't work?" Listen carefully. Are they dodging accountability or owning it? 4. "Can I talk to someone in my industry you've worked with?" Not a case study. An actual person I can call. If they hesitate, ask yourself why. 5. "What would make you tell me NOT to do this project?" This one's my favorite. It separates the order-takers from the advisors. Someone focused on your outcome will have a clear answer. Someone focused on their invoice will stumble. I've used these questions myself when hiring help for my own business. They've saved me from at least three expensive mistakes. The right partner welcomes these questions. The wrong one will try to talk around them. That's your signal. #HiringAdvice #BusinessConsulting #SmallBusiness #DueDiligence #BusinessOwner #Entrepreneurship #Leadership #BusinessTips

Integrated vs. fragmented solutions

One for automation. One for SEO. One to "integrate" everything. Each one bills separately. Each one points fingers when things go wrong. Each one optimizes for their piece without seeing the whole picture. I talked to a staffing agency owner last month who had this exact setup. His monthly retainer total: $14,000. His results? "I honestly can't tell you what's working." That's the real cost of fragmentation - not just money, but clarity. When your website team doesn't talk to your marketing team, you end up with beautiful pages that don't convert. When your automation vendor doesn't understand your sales process, you automate the wrong things. When everyone's responsible, no one's accountable. The alternative isn't always hiring one giant firm. Sometimes it's finding a partner who thinks about your entire growth system - not just their piece of it. Personal insight/lesson
I used to think the goal was to be the smartest person in the room. Took me 15 years to realize that was holding me back. When you position yourself as the expert with all the answers, a few things happen: 1. Clients don't tell you the full story (they're embarrassed to admit what they don't know) 2. You miss the obvious solutions (because you're looking for clever ones) 3. Relationships stay transactional (because expertise creates distance) The shift came when I started asking more than I told. "Walk me through how this process works today." "What's worked well before? What hasn't?" "What would success look like for you - in your words?" Suddenly, clients started opening up. They'd share the real problems - not the polished versions they thought I wanted to hear. And the solutions got simpler. Most of the time, business owners know what's broken. They just need someone to listen, confirm their instincts, and help them prioritize. The smartest thing I do now is create space for clients to be smart. Ask questions. Listen deeply. Reflect back what you hear. Then - and only then - add what you know. This lesson took me too long to learn. Hoping it saves you some time. #Leadership #BusinessWisdom #Consulting #PersonalGrowth #SmallBusiness #LessonsLearned #Entrepreneurship #Communication
