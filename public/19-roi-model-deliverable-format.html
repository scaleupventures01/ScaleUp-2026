Input Data Required from Client

Data collection framework for accurate ROI modeling

Accurate ROI modeling requires real data. We collect this during discovery through structured questionnaires and follow-up conversations. Never estimate when real data is available.

Financial Metrics

  • Current annual revenue
  • Revenue growth rate (last 2 years)
  • Average deal size / transaction value
  • Customer lifetime value (if known)
  • Customer acquisition cost
  • Current marketing/sales budget
  • Technology/tool spend

Team & Operations

  • Team size (by function)
  • Average hourly rate by role
  • Hours spent on manual processes
  • Current tool/system stack
  • Bottlenecks and pain points
  • Growth hiring plans
  • Remote vs. in-office distribution

Performance Data

  • Monthly lead volume
  • Lead-to-customer conversion rate
  • Website traffic (monthly)
  • Email list size
  • Social following
  • Current marketing channels
  • Top-performing content/campaigns

Goals & Constraints

  • Revenue goal (12 months)
  • Primary growth objective
  • Timeline urgency factors
  • Budget range (if disclosed)
  • Decision-maker approval process
  • Previous failed initiatives
  • Non-negotiable requirements
Data Quality Standard

If client cannot provide accurate data for a required metric, we use industry benchmarks with clear disclosure. Never present estimates as facts. The model's credibility depends on transparent sourcing.

Calculation Methodology

The mathematical framework behind our ROI projections

Our ROI calculations follow a rigorous, transparent methodology. Every number in the model traces back to either client-provided data, industry benchmarks, or our historical performance data.

Core ROI Formula
ROI = ((Total Benefits - Total Investment) / Total Investment) x 100
Simple, auditable, universally understood

Benefit Calculation Components

+

Revenue Increase

Formula:

(New Leads x Conversion Rate x Avg Deal Size) - Current Revenue from Same Channels

Based on projected lead volume improvements and maintained or improved conversion rates.

+

Cost Savings

Formula:

(Hours Saved x Hourly Rate) + (Tool Consolidation Savings) + (Reduced Error Costs)

Based on automation efficiencies, process improvements, and technology optimization.

+

Avoided Costs

Formula:

(Hiring Avoided x Annual Salary) + (Agency Fees Eliminated) + (Opportunity Costs)

Based on capacity created without headcount and service consolidation.

-

Investment Total

Formula:

ScaleUp Fees + Implementation Time (Client Hours x Rate) + Additional Tools/Resources

Fully-loaded cost including client time investment for accurate total cost of ownership.

Calculation Standards

  • ✓
    Conservative Baselines
    When using benchmarks, always use lower quartile performance, not averages or best cases.
  • ✓
    Time Value of Money
    For multi-year projections, apply appropriate discount rate (default 8%) to future cash flows.
  • ✓
    Ramp-Up Period
    First 60-90 days assume reduced effectiveness as implementations mature. No hockey sticks from day one.
  • ✓
    Margin-Based Revenue
    For revenue impact, consider gross margin not just top-line. A $10K sale at 30% margin = $3K benefit.
  • ✓
    Source Documentation
    Every number has a source: client-provided, ScaleUp historical, or third-party benchmark with citation.

Scenario Definitions

C

Conservative Scenario

When This Applies:

  • Implementation takes 25% longer than planned
  • Team adoption at 70% of expected
  • Conversion improvements at lower end of benchmarks
  • Market conditions slightly unfavorable

Probability: 20% chance of results at this level or lower

E

Expected Scenario

When This Applies:

  • Implementation on schedule
  • Team adoption at planned levels
  • Performance matches similar client outcomes
  • Standard market conditions

Probability: 60% chance of results at this level

O

Optimistic Scenario

When This Applies:

  • Implementation ahead of schedule
  • Team exceeds adoption targets
  • Conversion improvements at high end
  • Favorable market tailwinds

Probability: 20% chance of results at this level or higher

Scenario Presentation Guidance

Always lead with the Expected scenario. Present Conservative as "even in a challenging situation" and Optimistic as "if everything clicks." Never promise Optimistic as the baseline.

Presentation Guide for Delivering the Model

How to present the ROI model for maximum impact and buy-in

The ROI model presentation is as important as the model itself. This is where we convert analytical work into client confidence. Follow this structure for consistent, professional delivery.

1

Set the Stage (2 minutes)

Remind them why we built this model: "You asked us to show you exactly what the return would look like before investing. Here's what we found."

2

Executive Summary First (3 minutes)

Lead with the headline numbers: ROI percentage, payback period, Year 1 value. Let these sink in before going deeper. This is the "certainty" they came for.

3

Walk Through Their Data (5 minutes)

Show them the inputs we used from their business. This validates that we understand their situation and builds trust in the output.

4

Explain the Value Creation Logic (7 minutes)

Walk through how the investment creates value. Make it intuitive: "When we do X, it produces Y, which generates Z in value." Connect activities to outcomes.

5

Present the Scenarios (5 minutes)

Show all three scenarios. Emphasize that even Conservative is positive ROI. This demonstrates our confidence while acknowledging real-world variability.

6

Address Assumptions Proactively (5 minutes)

Walk through key assumptions before they ask. This builds credibility: "We assumed X - is that aligned with your experience?" Let them validate or adjust.

7

Questions and Sensitivity Exploration (10 minutes)

Open the interactive model. Let them ask "what if" questions and show them how the numbers change. This creates ownership of the projections.

8

Close with Next Steps (3 minutes)

End with clear action items: "Based on what we've shown, are you ready to move forward? Here's what happens next..."

Presentation Do's and Don'ts

Do

  • Send PDF in advance for review
  • Have interactive model ready for live exploration
  • Acknowledge uncertainty openly
  • Connect numbers to their specific goals
  • Let them challenge assumptions
  • Reference similar client outcomes

Don't

  • Oversell the optimistic scenario
  • Get defensive about assumptions
  • Promise guaranteed results
  • Rush through the methodology
  • Ignore concerns or objections
  • Skip the data validation step
