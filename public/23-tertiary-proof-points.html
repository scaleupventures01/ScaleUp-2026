How to Cite and Use Each Statistic

Usage Guidelines: Proper citation builds credibility. Follow these guidelines to accurately represent statistics across all marketing materials.

Citation Formats by Channel

Website Copy
Use inline citations with linked sources. Keep statistics prominent but sourced.
"97% of consumers use the internet to find local services."
Source: BrightLocal, 2024
Sales Proposals
Include full source information with access dates for credibility.
According to BrightLocal's 2024 Local Consumer Review Survey, 97% of consumers now use the internet to find local services. (Source: brightlocal.com/research/local-consumer-review-survey, accessed January 2025)
Social Media
Shortened attribution is acceptable for character-limited platforms.
Did you know? 97% of consumers search online for local services. Your digital presence IS your first impression. #LocalSEO
[Via BrightLocal]
Blog Content
Use hyperlinked citations with context for reader understanding.
A recent study by BrightLocal found that 97% of consumers use the internet to find local services, making digital visibility essential for any local business.

Best Practices for Statistical Claims

1 Always verify currency: Statistics older than 2 years should be refreshed or noted as historical.
2 Provide context: Raw percentages without explanation can mislead. Always explain what the statistic means.
3 Link to original sources: When possible, link directly to the study rather than secondary sources.
4 Use round numbers carefully: "Nearly 60%" is acceptable; "exactly 58.7%" requires precise sourcing.
5 Avoid cherry-picking: Present statistics that represent genuine industry consensus, not outliers.
6 Note methodology differences: Survey data differs from behavioral data. Be clear about data type.

Qualifying Language Guide

For survey-based statistics: "According to a survey by [Source]..." or "[X%] of respondents reported..."

For analytical/behavioral data: "[Source] found that..." or "Data from [Source] shows..."

For estimates/projections: "[Source] estimates..." or "Industry projections suggest..."

For older but relevant data: "A [Year] study by [Source] found..." or "Historically, [statistic]..."

Methodology Notes

* Survey data: Based on self-reported responses; may contain response bias. Sample sizes vary by study.
* Behavioral data: Based on actual observed behavior (search patterns, click data); generally more reliable than survey data.
* Industry estimates: Based on analyst models and public data; should be presented as estimates, not facts.
* Geographic scope: Most statistics are U.S.-focused unless otherwise noted.
