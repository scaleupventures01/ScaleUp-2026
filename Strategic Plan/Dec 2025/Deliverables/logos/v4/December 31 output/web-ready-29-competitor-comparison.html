Option 1

Option 2

Freelancers & Solopreneurs

Independent specialists who can move fast and cost less. Great for specific tasks, challenging for full strategies.

Freelancer Advantages

  • Lower hourly/project rates
  • Direct communication (no middlemen)
  • Flexible engagement terms
  • Deep expertise in one area
  • Fast turnaround on tasks

Freelancer Challenges

  • You become the project manager
  • No strategic oversight or integration
  • Availability gaps (vacations, other clients)
  • Single point of failure
  • Quality varies wildly

The Real Cost of Managing Freelancers

When you hire 3-4 freelancers for different functions (copy, design, ads, SEO), you spend 8-12 hours/week coordinating. At a CEO's time value of $200-500/hour, that's $1,600-6,000/week in hidden management costs - often more than a full-service partner would charge.

The Honest Verdict

Freelancers excel at defined, discrete tasks when you have the bandwidth to manage them. They struggle with integrated strategy, and the coordination burden falls on you.

Choose freelancers if:

You have clear project specs, time to manage, one-off needs (logo, website, single campaign), or internal strategy already set.

Consider ScaleUp if:

You need someone who can validate whether a project is worth doing before you spend on execution.

Go in-house if:

Option 5

DIY / No External Help

Handle everything internally. Bootstrap approach that works for some - but carries hidden costs most don't calculate.

The Hidden Costs of DIY Marketing

  • Founder time cost: 15-20 hours/week on marketing = $3,000-10,000/week in opportunity cost (deals not closed, product not improved, team not led)
  • Learning curve tax: 3-6 months to learn what specialists already know. Every mistake costs you money and time.
  • Tool sprawl: HubSpot, Mailchimp, Canva Pro, SEMrush, etc. = $500-2,000/month for tools you use at 20% capacity.
  • Wasted ad spend: Poorly optimized campaigns waste 40-60% of budget. $5,000/month in ads = $2,000-3,000 lost.
  • Competitor speed: While you're learning, funded competitors with agencies are taking market share.

What You Miss Without Expert Guidance

  • Cross-industry pattern recognition
  • Benchmark data from similar companies
  • Vendor relationships and rates
  • Early warning on platform changes
  • Objective outside perspective
  • Accountability to real numbers

The Honest Verdict

DIY often makes sense for bootstrapped founders, especially in early stages. The question is whether you're spending time on validation vs. execution. Sometimes a small engagement to validate an idea saves months of building the wrong thing.

Stay DIY if:

You're pre-revenue bootstrapping, have genuine expertise yourself, or are learning by doing as part of your founder journey.

Consider ScaleUp if:

You want validation before building (we've told clients NOT to build and saved them $150K+), or need a regulated-industry POC.

12 Questions to Ask Any Agency Before Hiring

Use this checklist to vet any marketing partner. A good agency will have clear, confident answers. Evasiveness is a red flag.

1

Who will actually do the work?

Get names and bios. If they can't tell you, it's probably junior staff or outsourced.

2

What metrics will you be accountable to?

Accept only business outcomes (revenue, leads, conversion). Reject vanity metrics (impressions, reach). Look for concrete ROI commitments like guaranteed lead numbers or three-scenario projections.

3

Can I see 3 case studies with measurable results?

Not logos or testimonials. Actual numbers: "We increased revenue by X% in Y months."

4

What's your minimum commitment?

12-month minimums protect the agency, not you. Look for 90-day pilots or month-to-month.

5

What's the exit clause if it's not working?

Get this in writing. 30-day notice with no penalty is fair. Big termination fees are red flags.

6

Who owns the work product and data?

You should own everything: creative assets, ad accounts, data, copy. If not, you're building their asset.

7

How do you handle strategy vs. execution?

Many agencies do one or the other. You need both integrated - or clear handoff plans.

8

What's your communication cadence?

Weekly calls minimum. Real-time access to dashboards. Named contact for urgent issues.

9

Have you worked in my industry before?

Industry experience matters less than you think - but they should understand your sales cycle and buyer.

10

What will you need from me?

Good agencies know what access, content, and time they need. Vague answers = scope creep later.

11

What happens when something fails?

Everything fails sometimes. You want a partner who diagnoses quickly and pivots, not one who makes excuses.

12

Can I talk to a current client?

Not a hand-picked testimonial. A current client you can ask hard questions. Refusal is a dealbreaker.
