The Situation

A Client Ready to Invest Six Figures in the Wrong Direction

A business owner came to us with a vision for a custom web application. They had budget allocated, a rough feature list, and were ready to start development. They hired ScaleUp for a $5,000 discovery engagement to validate the approach and create a development roadmap.

Most consultancies would have happily taken the project. The client was eager, funded, and asking for exactly what we offer. But "Certainty First" means telling the truth, even when the truth costs us a larger engagement.

The Discovery

When the Right Answer Is "Don't Build This"

Our discovery process revealed fundamental issues with the proposed application. The market assumptions underlying the product were flawed. The technical requirements would have required ongoing infrastructure costs the client hadn't anticipated. Most critically, existing off-the-shelf solutions could solve the core problem at a fraction of the cost.

We presented our findings honestly: building this application would be a mistake. The client would likely spend $150,000 or more and 12+ months only to discover what we found in two weeks of focused analysis.

  • 1Market Analysis: Evaluated competitive landscape and demand signals
  • 2Technical Assessment: Mapped architecture requirements and ongoing costs
  • 3Alternative Identification: Found existing solutions that addressed core needs
  • 4Honest Recommendation: Advised against the build despite losing larger project

The Challenge

Complex Domains Requiring Enterprise-Grade Thinking

This engagement required building a proof of concept that spanned financial services and medical technology, two of the most heavily regulated and complex domains in software development. The client needed to demonstrate feasibility before committing to a full build.

Regulated industries demand a different approach. Security isn't an afterthought. Compliance isn't optional. Every architectural decision must account for audit trails, data protection, and regulatory requirements that most developers never encounter.

The Approach

Enterprise Thinking from Day One

We approached this POC with the rigor of a production system. The goal wasn't just to prove technical feasibility, but to demonstrate that the solution could operate in regulated environments with enterprise-grade security requirements.

  • 1Regulatory Mapping: Identified compliance requirements across both domains
  • 2Security Architecture: Designed with enterprise security considerations throughout
  • 3Technical Validation: Proved core functionality could work within constraints
  • 4Path Forward: Documented requirements for production implementation
