Marketing Speak to Avoid

The problem with marketing jargon: After 30+ years in business, our clients have heard every buzzword. They're skeptical of hype and can smell insincerity immediately. Our job is to communicate value clearly, not dress up ordinary things in fancy language.

Avoid This Say This Instead Why It Matters Category
Synergy Work well together, Combined strength This word has been mocked for decades. Using it unironically damages credibility. Marketing
Paradigm shift Major change, New way of doing things If something is truly revolutionary, describe what actually changed. Marketing
Disruptive New approach, Different, Changes how things work Every startup claims to be "disruptive." The word has lost all meaning. Marketing
Best-in-class Top-quality, Excellent, [Specific proof point] Empty claim without evidence. Instead: "Rated #1 by [Source]" or describe the specific advantage. Marketing
World-class Excellent, Outstanding, [Specific proof] Same issue as "best-in-class." Prove it with specifics or don't claim it. Marketing
Solutions [Name the specific thing], Services, Help "Solutions" is vague. Say what you actually provide: "accounting help," "marketing services," "business coaching." Marketing
Value proposition What you get, The benefit, Why it matters Internal planning term, not client-facing language. Just explain the value. Marketing
Touch base Talk, Meet, Check in, Catch up Corporate-speak that feels impersonal. "Let's talk next week" is warmer. Marketing
Circle back Get back to you, Follow up, Discuss later Another corporate cliche. Be direct about what happens next. Marketing
Move the needle Make a real difference, Get results Be specific: "Increase sales by 15%" rather than "move the needle on sales." Marketing
Low-hanging fruit Quick wins, Easy improvements, Good first step This metaphor is overused. Name the actual opportunity. Marketing
Game-changer Major improvement, Big difference Overused to the point of meaninglessness. Describe the actual impact. Marketing
Thought leader Expert, Authority, Experienced professional Self-applying this term sounds arrogant. Let credentials and work speak. Marketing
Bandwidth Time, Capacity, Availability "Do you have time for this?" not "Do you have bandwidth?" Marketing
Pivot Change direction, Adjust, Shift focus Startup buzzword. For established businesses: "We're shifting our focus to..." Marketing

Industry-Specific Jargon Translations

Our clients come from many industries. While they know their own industry deeply, they may not understand consulting, finance, or tech terminology. Here's how to translate specialized language into everyday English.

Acronyms That Need Explanation

The rule for acronyms: Spell it out the first time, every time. Even if you think they know it, define it. Our clients will never feel embarrassed to ask, but they might feel excluded if we assume knowledge they don't have.

API Tech
Application Programming Interface - A way for different software programs to talk to each other.
Say this instead
"Your systems can share information automatically."
B2B / B2C Business
Business-to-Business / Business-to-Consumer - Who you sell to: other businesses or individual customers.
Say this instead
"Businesses that sell to other businesses" or "Businesses that sell directly to customers."
CPA Marketing
Cost Per Acquisition - How much you spend to get one new customer. (Not to be confused with Certified Public Accountant!)
Say this instead
"It costs you $50 to acquire each new customer."
SEO Digital
Search Engine Optimization - Making your website easier for Google to find and show to searchers.
Say this instead
"Helping your website show up when people search Google for what you offer."
SOP Operations
Standard Operating Procedure - Written instructions for how to do recurring tasks.
Say this instead
"Step-by-step instructions that anyone can follow."
P&L Finance
Profit & Loss Statement - A report showing what you earned and spent over a period.
Say this instead
"Your income and expense report" (Most business owners know this one, but define it anyway.)
ERP Software
Enterprise Resource Planning - Big software that manages many parts of a business (inventory, accounting, HR, etc.).
Say this instead
"All-in-one business software" or name the specific system (SAP, Oracle, etc.).
UX / UI Design
User Experience / User Interface - How easy software is to use (UX) and what it looks like (UI).
Say this instead
"How easy it is to use" or "The look and feel."
MRR / ARR Finance
Monthly/Annual Recurring Revenue - Predictable income from subscriptions or contracts.
Say this instead
"Money that comes in every month (or year) on autopilot."
CAC Marketing
Customer Acquisition Cost - Total cost to acquire a new customer, including marketing and sales.
Say this instead
"What it costs you to get a new customer."
LTV Business
Lifetime Value - Total money a customer will spend with you over the entire relationship.
Say this instead
"What a customer is worth to you over time."
YoY Finance
Year over Year - Comparing this year's numbers to the same period last year.
Say this instead
"Compared to the same time last year."

Before/After Examples of Jargon Removal

Learning by example: These real-world transformations show how to maintain professionalism while speaking clearly. Notice how the "after" versions are often shorter, more specific, and more actionable.

Email Communication

Before
"I wanted to touch base regarding our strategic alignment on the digital transformation initiative. We should circle back on the key learnings from last quarter and ideate on ways to move the needle on our KPIs."
After
"I wanted to talk with you about updating your accounting and inventory systems. Let's review what worked well last quarter and come up with ideas to improve your numbers."

Service Description

Before
"ScaleUp Ventures provides end-to-end business solutions that leverage cutting-edge technology to drive digital transformation. Our holistic approach ensures seamless integration across your ecosystem."
After
"ScaleUp Ventures helps you get your business ready for what's next. We update your systems, train your team, and make sure everything works together. Think of us as your guide through growth."

Proposal Text

Before
"Phase 1 deliverables include a comprehensive audit of your tech stack, stakeholder alignment sessions, and a roadmap for optimizing operational efficiency. ROI projections indicate significant value creation within 18 months."
After
"In the first phase, we'll review all your software and systems, meet with your key people to understand priorities, and create a step-by-step plan for running things more smoothly. Based on similar projects, you can expect to save $75,000 or more within 18 months."

Website Copy

Before
"Unlock synergies and scale your business with our best-in-class platform. Our AI-powered solutions disrupt traditional business models while maximizing your value proposition."
After
"Grow your business with proven strategies that work for companies like yours. We use smart technology to save you time and help you stay ahead of competitors without the headaches."

Explaining Technology

Before
"We'll implement a cloud-based CRM with API integrations to your existing ERP. This enables real-time data synchronization and automated workflows that scale with your business."
After
"We'll set up a customer tracking system (like Salesforce) that connects to your accounting software. When a sale happens, your inventory updates automatically. No more double-entry. And the system handles more business as you grow."

How to Handle Unavoidable Technical Terms

Sometimes technical terms are necessary. When a concept genuinely requires specific terminology, use these techniques to introduce it clearly without talking down to your client.

The 4-Step Translation Framework

  1. Lead with the benefit or outcome
    Start with what they care about, not what the thing is called. "This will let your customers pay online 24/7."
  2. Introduce the term naturally
    Give it a name if needed: "This is called a payment gateway - it's what connects your website to the bank."
  3. Use a relatable comparison
    Connect to something they know: "Think of it like a cash register, but for online sales."
  4. Circle back to their world
    Connect back to their business: "So when someone orders from your website at midnight, the money still ends up in your account."

Techniques for Explaining Complex Concepts

Technique How It Works Example
The Analogy Bridge Connect new concepts to familiar experiences from their industry or daily life. "A database is like a very organized filing cabinet that finds any file instantly."
The "Which Means" Connector Follow every technical term with its practical implication. "We'll set up automated backups, which means if anything goes wrong, we can restore your data from yesterday."
The Cost/Benefit Translation Express technical features in terms of money or time saved. "Integration saves your bookkeeper about 3 hours per week of manual entry."
The "In Other Words" Pause Introduce the technical term, then immediately translate. "We'll implement two-factor authentication - in other words, a second security step to make sure it's really you logging in."
The Specific Example Replace abstract concepts with concrete scenarios. Instead of "scalable infrastructure," say "When you have 50 orders a day instead of 10, the system handles it without slowing down."
The Question Check Pause and invite clarification without condescension. "Does that make sense, or would it help if I walked through a specific example?"

Copy Audit Checklist

Use this checklist before publishing any client-facing content. Every email, proposal, website page, and presentation should pass this jargon audit. Print it out. Keep it visible. Make it habit.

Pre-Publication Jargon Audit

  • Read aloud test
    Read the content aloud. Does it sound like how you'd explain this to a neighbor at a barbecue?
  • Acronym check
    Are all acronyms spelled out on first use? Would someone unfamiliar with our industry understand them?
  • Buzzword sweep
    Scan for: leverage, optimize, synergy, paradigm, disrupt, ecosystem, holistic, scalable, robust, cutting-edge
  • Corporate-speak elimination
    Remove: circle back, touch base, move the needle, low-hanging fruit, value-add, bandwidth, deliverables
  • Tech jargon translation
    Replace or explain: cloud, API, integration, platform, SaaS, AI-powered, machine learning, automation
  • Specific over vague
    Replace vague claims with specifics: "significant improvement" becomes "15% increase" or "saves 3 hours weekly"
  • Client perspective check
    Imagine a 60-year-old business owner reading this. Would they feel respected? Informed? Or confused and talked-down-to?
  • Benefit vs. feature balance
    For every feature mentioned, is there a clear "which means..." benefit the client cares about?
  • Action clarity
    Is it clear what the client should do next? Are instructions in simple, numbered steps?
  • Second reader test
    Have someone outside the project read it. Can they summarize the main point in one sentence?

The Ultimate Test

Ask yourself: "Am I using this word because it's the clearest way to explain this, or because it makes me sound smart?"

If it's the latter, find a simpler word. Our clients don't need to be impressed by our vocabulary. They need to understand how we can help them.

Internal Team Glossary

For internal use only. This glossary helps our team understand industry terms so we can translate them for clients. Knowing what something means is the first step to explaining it simply.

Churn Rate Business
The percentage of customers who stop using a service over a given period. High churn means customers are leaving faster than you can replace them.
For clients
"The percentage of customers who don't come back each month."
Gross Margin Finance
Revenue minus cost of goods sold, expressed as a percentage. Measures profitability before overhead expenses.
For clients
"How much you keep from each sale after paying for the product itself."
Unit Economics Strategy
The direct revenues and costs associated with a single unit (customer, product, or transaction). Fundamental for understanding profitability.
For clients
"How much you make (or lose) on each sale or customer."
COGS Finance
Cost of Goods Sold - direct costs attributable to producing the goods sold by a company.
For clients
"What you pay to buy or make the products you sell."
Pipeline Sales
The total value of potential sales opportunities at various stages of the sales process.
For clients
"All the potential sales you're working on, from first conversation to almost-closed."
Sprint Project
A fixed time period (usually 1-4 weeks) during which specific work must be completed and ready for review.
For clients
"A focused work period - usually 2 weeks - where we complete a specific set of tasks."
MVP Product
Minimum Viable Product - the simplest version of a product that can be released to test a market hypothesis.
For clients
"The simplest version we can build first, to test if customers want it before investing more."
Scope Creep Project
Uncontrolled changes or continuous growth in a project's scope, often leading to delays and budget overruns.
For clients
"When a project keeps growing as we add 'just one more thing' - which is why we're careful about defining what's included upfront."
Technical Debt Tech
The implied cost of additional work caused by choosing an easy (limited) solution now instead of a better approach that would take longer.
For clients
"Quick fixes that work now but create extra work later - like patching a roof instead of replacing it."
Onboarding Customer
The process of integrating a new customer or employee and giving them the tools, knowledge, and access they need.
For clients
"Getting you set up and comfortable with how we work together."

Client-Facing Glossary

Optional resource for clients. This simplified glossary can be shared with clients who want a reference for terms they might encounter. It's written in their language, not ours.

A Note for Our Clients

Business is full of jargon. Here's a quick reference for terms you might hear from us, other consultants, software vendors, or industry publications. If you ever encounter a word we use that's not on this list - or that still doesn't make sense - please ask. We'd rather explain something twice than have you feel uncertain.

Backup Technology
A copy of your important files stored separately, so if something goes wrong with your computer or system, you don't lose everything. Like having a spare key for your house.
Dashboard Reports
A screen that shows your most important numbers at a glance - like the dashboard in your car shows speed and fuel level. Helps you check on your business quickly.
Login / Password Security
Your personal "key" to enter an online system. Your login is usually your email address; your password is a secret code only you should know.
Subscription Payments
Paying monthly or yearly for something instead of buying it once. Like a magazine subscription, but for software or services. Cancel anytime (usually).
Update / Upgrade Software
Update: Fixes and small improvements (usually free).
Upgrade: A major new version with new features (may cost extra).
Download / Upload Internet
Download: Getting something from the internet to your computer (like receiving a package).
Upload: Sending something from your computer to the internet (like mailing a package).
App Technology
Short for "application" - a program that does something specific. Like the apps on your phone (Facebook, Weather, Maps), but also includes software on computers.
Invoice Business
A bill you send to customers telling them what they owe and when to pay. Includes details of what was provided and the total amount due.

Training Guide for New Team Members

Welcome to ScaleUp Ventures. One of the most important skills you'll develop here is communicating complex ideas simply. This training will help you speak like us: clearly, warmly, and without jargon.

Module 1: Why Plain Language Matters Here

Our clients are successful business owners aged 50-65. They've built companies through grit, instinct, and experience - not by memorizing buzzwords. Many feel intimidated by technology and marketing speak, though they'd never admit it.

Our job is to be their trusted guide, not to impress them with our vocabulary.

Using jargon creates distance. Using plain language builds trust. Every word choice is either bringing us closer to our clients or pushing them away.

Exercise 1

Find a piece of content you wrote at a previous job. Highlight every word that a 60-year-old plumber might not immediately understand. Then rewrite the whole piece using only words they would know.

Module 2: The "Neighbor Test"

Before sending any client communication, apply the "Neighbor Test":

Imagine you're explaining this to your neighbor at a weekend barbecue. They're smart, but they don't work in your industry. How would you explain it to them?

If your written version sounds different from that conversation, rewrite it.

Exercise 2

Take this sentence and rewrite it for the neighbor test:

"We'll leverage our proprietary methodology to optimize your go-to-market strategy and drive sustainable revenue growth through enhanced customer acquisition frameworks."

Module 3: Translating On The Fly

In meetings, you'll sometimes hear colleagues, vendors, or clients themselves use jargon. When a client uses a term, don't assume they know what it means - they may have heard it from someone else.

Safe ways to check understanding:

  • "Just to make sure we're on the same page, when you say [term], you mean...?"
  • "That's a great point. So in practical terms, that would mean...?"
  • "Let me make sure I understand what you're looking for..."
Exercise 3

Role-play: A client says "We need better SEO and maybe some kind of CRM that integrates with our ERP." Practice responding in a way that clarifies what they actually need without making them feel uninformed.

Module 4: Words to Eliminate From Your Vocabulary

Commit these to memory. When you catch yourself writing or saying these, stop and find a simpler alternative:

leverage optimize synergy ecosystem scalable holistic robust solutions paradigm disruptive touch base circle back
Exercise 4

Review the last 10 emails you've sent. Count how many of these words you used. Set a goal to use zero in your next 10 emails.

Module 5: Ongoing Practice

Weekly habit: Pick one piece of external content (competitor website, industry article, vendor proposal) and "translate" it into ScaleUp Ventures language.

Monthly review: Have a colleague read your recent client communications and flag any jargon that slipped through.

Quarterly audit: Review this entire document and refresh your awareness of terms to avoid.

Final Thought

The mark of true expertise is the ability to explain complex things simply. When you find yourself reaching for jargon, pause and ask: "Do I actually understand this well enough to explain it to my neighbor?" If not, keep learning until you do.
