When NOT to Build: The Most Valuable Advice I Give

By Calvin Williams Jr. | January 2, 2025 | 6 min read

The most valuable advice I give founders is not about what to build. It is about what not to build. And it is advice most of them do not want to hear.

Over the years, I have sat across from countless founders and product managers who were excited about their next big idea. They had the vision. They had the enthusiasm. Sometimes they even had the funding. What they often did not have was a reason to build.

This is the advice I wish someone had given me earlier in my career: sometimes the best product decision is no product at all.

The Build Bias

We are trained to build. As product managers, as founders, as technologists, building is what we do. When we see a problem, our instinct is to create a solution. When a customer has a pain point, we imagine the feature that would solve it.

This instinct is valuable, but it can also be dangerous. Building is expensive. It consumes time, money, and attention. Every feature you build is a feature you must maintain, support, and explain. Every product you launch is a product you must sell, deliver, and improve.

The question is not whether you can build something. The question is whether you should.

The Questions That Matter

Before any build decision, I walk through a series of questions. These are not meant to kill ideas. They are meant to ensure that when we do build, we build the right thing for the right reasons.

Is there an existing solution?

The world is full of software. Before you build, look hard at what already exists. Can you buy instead of build? Can you partner? Can you integrate? The cost of building from scratch is almost always higher than it appears, and the cost of maintaining what you build is often higher still.

Is the problem worth solving?

Not every problem deserves a solution. Some problems are too small to justify the investment. Some problems sound important but do not actually drive behavior. Some problems affect too few people to build a sustainable business around them.

I have seen teams spend months building elegant solutions to problems that, when you traced the impact, affected almost nobody's bottom line.

Can you validate without building?

Before you commit to building, what can you learn without writing code? Can you mock it up and show it to potential users? Can you run a landing page test to gauge interest? Can you deliver the service manually before you automate it?

The goal is to find the cheapest, fastest way to test your core assumptions. Building is the expensive test. It should come after the cheap tests, not before.

What happens if you do nothing?

This is the question people skip. If you do not build this, what happens? Sometimes the answer is catastrophic: you lose customers, miss a market, fail to meet commitments. But often the answer is surprisingly benign: things continue roughly as they are.

If doing nothing has low consequences, that is information worth having.

A Real Example

I once worked with a team that was convinced they needed a complex new feature. The request had come from sales, who said customers were asking for it. The pressure was intense.

Before we started building, we did the work. We talked to the customers who had supposedly requested it. We asked them to show us their workflow. We asked what they would do if the feature did not exist.

What we found was that the "customer request" was actually one person on one call mentioning something that would be "nice to have." When we dug into their actual workflow, they had a manual workaround that took about ten minutes per week. Building the feature would have taken months.

We did not build it. The customer was fine. The sales team moved on. We spent those months on something that actually mattered.

When to Build

This is not an argument against building. Building is how products improve and businesses grow. The argument is for building deliberately, with clear eyes about why you are building and what you expect to get from it.

Build when:

  • The problem is validated and significant. Real users have real pain that is costing them real time or money.
  • No adequate solution exists. You have genuinely looked and confirmed that buying or partnering is not a better option.
  • The economics work. You have modeled the cost of building and maintaining against the value you expect to capture, and the math makes sense.
  • You have conviction. Not just enthusiasm, but reasoned confidence based on evidence and analysis.

The Certainty Principle

At ScaleUp Ventures, we built our practice around a simple principle: certainty first. Before we recommend building anything, we do the analysis. We validate the assumptions. We model the economics. We stress test the logic.

Sometimes that analysis leads to a confident build recommendation. Sometimes it leads to a confident no-build recommendation. Either way, you know what you are getting into before you start spending.

The most expensive product is the one you should not have built in the first place. The most valuable advice is often the advice that saves you from that mistake.

The ROI Model Revolution: How to Know Before You Spend

By Calvin Williams Jr. | January 9, 2025 | 7 min read

What if you could see the outcome of your investment before you made it? What if, instead of hoping your next initiative would work, you could know with reasonable confidence whether the math made sense?

This is not a fantasy. It is a discipline. And it is available to every business owner willing to demand more than promises from their partners.

The Old Way Is Gambling

Traditional business development works like this: someone proposes an initiative. They give you a budget. They make predictions based on industry averages and their own experience. You write a check. Then you wait months to see if it worked.

By the time you know whether the investment paid off, you have already spent the money. If it failed, you are out the cash and back to square one. If it succeeded, you still do not fully understand why, which makes repeating the success difficult.

This approach treats business investment like gambling. You place your bet, spin the wheel, and hope for the best. It is exciting for service providers who collect their fees regardless of outcome. It is terrifying for business owners who are betting their capital.

What an ROI Model Actually Does

An ROI model flips this script. Instead of spending first and measuring later, you model first and spend only when the math works.

Here is how it works in practice:

Step 1: Define Your Economics

Before any initiative is designed, we need to understand your business model at a granular level. What is your average customer worth? Not just on the first transaction, but over time. What are your margins? What is your capacity? If you suddenly got fifty new customers next month, could you actually serve them?

These questions matter because they set the boundaries of what any investment can accomplish. If the math does not work at the fundamental level, no amount of execution excellence will make the initiative profitable.

Step 2: Map the Value Chain

Every business has a path from initial investment to realized value. For some, it is simple. For others, it involves weeks or months of activities, multiple touchpoints, and complex dependencies.

Understanding your specific value chain lets us identify where investment can add the most leverage. Maybe you do not need more leads. Maybe you need better conversion. Maybe your problem is not acquisition at all but retention.

Step 3: Build the Model

With your economics and value chain mapped, we can build a mathematical model of your proposed investment. This model includes:

  • Expected inputs based on your budget and approach
  • Estimated conversion rates using benchmarks adjusted for your specific situation
  • Projected value generation at each stage of your process
  • Calculated cost per outcome
  • Compared against your expected value

The output is simple: for every dollar you spend, here is what we expect to get back. And here are the assumptions that drive that expectation.

Step 4: Stress Test

Every model has assumptions, and assumptions can be wrong. So we stress test. What if conversion rates are half what we expect? What if your team executes at 75 percent instead of 100 percent? What if external factors shift?

By testing the model against pessimistic scenarios, we identify the breaking points. You will know exactly what needs to be true for the initiative to work, and how much room for error exists.

Why Most Advisors Do Not Do This

If ROI modeling is so valuable, why does not every advisor do it? The answer is uncomfortable: because it often leads to spending less, not more.

When you actually run the numbers, many proposed initiatives do not make financial sense. The cost is too high. The margins are too thin. The opportunity is too small. Whatever the reason, the math says no.

For an advisor who makes money by selling services, this is a problem. Every modeled initiative that does not launch is revenue they do not collect. So they avoid the discipline entirely, preferring optimistic projections and vague promises to rigorous analysis.

What Changes When You Demand Models

When you require ROI modeling before any investment, several things shift:

You stop wasting money on bad bets. Initiatives that would have failed get caught before they launch. The money you would have lost stays in your account.

You focus resources where they matter. When you can see which initiatives have the highest expected return, you can allocate accordingly. Instead of spreading budget across everything, you concentrate on what works.

You learn faster. Because you know the assumptions going in, you can track whether they held true. When something outperforms the model, you understand why. When something underperforms, you can identify which assumption was wrong and adjust.

You sleep better. There is a particular anxiety that comes from spending money when you have no idea if it will work. ROI modeling does not eliminate all uncertainty, but it replaces blind hope with informed confidence.

The Certainty Standard

At ScaleUp Ventures, we made ROI modeling the foundation of every client relationship. Before we propose any initiative, we build the model. We share it with you. We explain every assumption. And we only recommend moving forward when the expected return justifies the investment.

Sometimes that means we do less than a client expected. Sometimes it means we do different things than they imagined. Occasionally it means we recommend doing nothing at all.

But it always means you know what you are getting into before you spend. That is the certainty standard, and it is what every business owner deserves.

5 Signs You're About to Waste Money on a New Product

By Calvin Williams Jr. | January 16, 2025 | 5 min read

In my years advising founders and product teams, I have seen significant resources wasted on products that never had a chance of working. The frustrating part? The warning signs were there from the beginning.

Before you approve that next product initiative or greenlight that new build, check for these red flags. Any one of them should give you pause. Multiple flags? Step back and reconsider.

Sign #1: You Cannot Explain the Problem

Ask yourself a simple question: what specific problem does this product solve, and for whom?

Not a vague opportunity. Not a general market trend. A specific, painful problem that specific people have.

If you cannot articulate this clearly, you are about to build a solution looking for a problem. I have seen teams spend months building products that were technically impressive but solved problems nobody actually had.

Watch out for these deflection patterns:

  • "Everyone will want this" - No they will not. Who specifically?
  • "The market is huge" - Market size does not mean problem size
  • "It is the future" - Maybe, but does anyone need it today?
  • "Competitors are doing it" - That does not mean it is working for them

Sign #2: You Have Not Talked to Users

How many potential users have you actually spoken with about this problem? Not surveyed. Not sent a form to. Actually spoken with, in enough depth to understand their real workflow and pain points.

If the answer is fewer than ten, you do not have enough information to build. If the answer is zero, stop immediately.

Building products based on assumptions about what users want is how you create beautifully designed solutions that nobody uses. The only way to avoid this is to get out of the building and talk to real people.

Sign #3: You Cannot Articulate the No-Build Alternative

What happens if you do not build this? What are users doing today to solve this problem?

If users have no current solution, that might mean you have found a gap in the market. Or it might mean the problem is not painful enough to solve. Which one is it?

If users have workarounds, what are they? Spreadsheets? Manual processes? Competitor products? Understanding the current state tells you what you are really competing against and what your product needs to be meaningfully better than.

The inability to describe the alternative is a sign that you have not studied the problem deeply enough to solve it well.

Sign #4: The Timeline Is Aggressive

How confident are you in the launch timeline? If the answer is "very confident," and you are building something new, you are probably wrong.

New products almost always take longer than expected. Requirements evolve. Technical challenges emerge. Integration is harder than anticipated. Testing reveals issues that need resolution.

A product timeline that has no buffer for learning and iteration is a timeline that will slip, or worse, ship something half-baked that damages your reputation.

Look for:

  • Fixed ship dates announced before development starts
  • No time allocated for user testing and iteration
  • Pressure to launch before the product is ready
  • Scope that assumes everything goes perfectly

Sign #5: Success Metrics Are Vague

How will you know if this product is successful? If the answer involves words like "adoption" or "engagement" without specific numbers, you are setting yourself up for failure that is hard to recognize.

Clear success metrics before you build serve two purposes: they force clarity about what you are actually trying to accomplish, and they give you a honest measure of whether you accomplished it.

Vague metrics let everyone claim success regardless of outcome. That feels good in the short term but prevents learning.

"Every product decision should start with a clear hypothesis about what success looks like. Only then can you know if you achieved it."

The Alternative Exists

I know this list can feel discouraging. If you have built products before, you may recognize some of these patterns from your own experience.

But there is another way. You can validate problems before building solutions. You can talk to users before writing code. You can set clear success metrics before launching. You can build iteratively rather than betting everything on one big launch.

This approach is slower at the start but faster overall, because it avoids the expensive detours that come from building the wrong thing.

What I Learned Supporting Founders Through the Startup Journey

By Calvin Williams Jr. | January 23, 2025 | 7 min read

Supporting founders is not like traditional consulting. Founders are not looking for a report they can file away. They are building something from nothing, often with limited resources and unlimited uncertainty. What they need is different.

Having worked alongside founders at various stages, from initial concept to product-market fit and beyond, I have learned some lessons about what actually helps.

The Loneliness Problem

Founding a company is isolating in ways that people outside the experience do not fully understand. You cannot share all your concerns with your team because they are looking to you for confidence. You cannot share everything with your investors because they are evaluating you. You cannot always share with your family because they are already worried about you.

One of the most valuable things an outside advisor can provide is simply a safe space to think out loud. Someone who understands the context, has no political stake in your decisions, and can be a sounding board without judgment.

This sounds soft. It is not. The decisions founders make when they are isolated and under pressure are often worse than the decisions they make when they have had a chance to talk through their thinking with someone who understands.

The Clarity Problem

Startups are chaotic. There are always more opportunities than resources, more fires than firefighters, more decisions than information to make them with.

In this environment, clarity becomes incredibly valuable. What are the two or three things that actually matter right now? What can wait? What should be actively ignored?

Founders often need help not with generating ideas but with ruthlessly prioritizing among them. The default state is trying to do too many things at once. The better state is doing fewer things with more focus.

This requires saying no. To good ideas. To interesting opportunities. To things that would be nice to have. Saying no is emotionally difficult, and having someone help you see what should be cut is often easier than cutting it yourself.

The Pattern Recognition Problem

Every startup feels unique to its founders. And in some ways it is. But many of the challenges follow patterns that repeat across companies and industries.

An advisor who has seen those patterns can help founders avoid common mistakes and recognize common opportunities. Not by telling them what to do, because their situation is indeed unique, but by sharing context about what has worked and not worked in similar circumstances.

This pattern recognition is not about having all the answers. It is about helping founders ask better questions and avoid obvious pitfalls.

What Actually Helps

Being Available

Startup challenges do not arrive on schedule. The crisis happens when it happens. The critical decision needs to be made this week, not during next month's scheduled check-in.

Being genuinely available when founders need help, not just when it is convenient, is more valuable than most formal advisory structures.

Being Honest

Founders are surrounded by people who have reasons to be less than fully honest. Employees want to be supportive. Investors want to stay positive. Customers have their own interests.

An advisor who will tell the hard truth, who will say "that strategy is not working" or "you need to make a change you are avoiding," provides something rare. Honest feedback is a gift, even when it is uncomfortable to receive.

Knowing Your Limits

Good advisors know what they know and what they do not. When a founder faces a challenge outside my expertise, the most valuable thing I can do is say so and help them find someone who can actually help.

Pretending to have expertise you lack is worse than useless in a startup context, where bad advice can have significant consequences.

What Does Not Help

Generic advice. "Focus on product-market fit" is not helpful. What specific actions should this founder take this week?

War stories. My experience is relevant only insofar as it illuminates their situation. Nobody needs to hear about my glory days.

Taking over. Founders need to own their decisions. An advisor who tries to make decisions for them is undermining rather than helping.

Disappearing. Sporadic engagement is often worse than no engagement. If you are going to be involved, be consistently involved.

The Partnership Model

The best founder-advisor relationships feel like partnerships. Not hierarchical, not transactional, but genuinely collaborative. The founder brings context, domain expertise, and decision-making authority. The advisor brings perspective, pattern recognition, and honest feedback.

Together, you can solve problems that neither could solve alone.

This kind of relationship takes time to build and requires trust on both sides. But when it works, it is one of the most rewarding forms of professional collaboration I know.

Building in Regulated Industries: Lessons from a Trading App

By Calvin Williams Jr. | January 30, 2025 | 7 min read

Regulated industries are different. The playbooks that work in consumer apps or B2B SaaS do not translate directly when you are building something that has to satisfy regulatory requirements alongside user needs.

I learned this deeply while working on a trading application. The experience taught me things about product development that I would not have learned anywhere else.

The Compliance Reality

When you are building in financial services, compliance is not a nice-to-have. It is an existential requirement. You do not get to ship now and fix later. You do not get to move fast and break things. You get to move carefully and document everything.

This changes your entire approach to product development. Every feature has to be evaluated not just for user value but for regulatory implications. Every design decision has to consider audit trails and reporting requirements. Every launch has to wait for compliance review.

For product managers coming from unregulated environments, this can feel like moving through molasses. But it is the reality of the industry, and fighting it just makes everything harder.

The Documentation Discipline

Regulated industries demand documentation that would seem excessive anywhere else. Why did you make this decision? What alternatives did you consider? Who approved it? When?

At first, this feels like bureaucratic overhead. Over time, I came to appreciate it as a discipline that actually improves decision-making.

When you know your decisions will be documented and potentially reviewed, you think more carefully about them. You consider more alternatives. You make fewer decisions on pure intuition and more on reasoned analysis.

The documentation is not just for regulators. It is institutional memory that helps the team understand why things are the way they are.

Building Trust with Regulators

Regulators are not the enemy. They are stakeholders with legitimate interests that you need to satisfy. Treating them as obstacles to work around is a recipe for problems.

The better approach is to build relationships before you need them. Engage early. Seek guidance. Show that you are taking compliance seriously. When issues do arise, and they will, you want regulators to believe you are acting in good faith.

This takes time and patience. It is not as exciting as shipping features. But it is essential infrastructure for anything that is going to last.

The User Experience Challenge

Here is the hard part: regulatory requirements often make user experience worse. Disclosures that users have to acknowledge. Verification steps that add friction. Limitations on what you can offer and how.

The temptation is to minimize these requirements, to hide them in small print or push them to the background. This is a mistake. Aside from the compliance risk, it damages trust when users feel surprised by requirements you tried to hide.

The better approach is to integrate compliance into the experience thoughtfully. Make required disclosures clear and understandable. Explain why verification is necessary. Turn regulatory requirements into signals that this is a serious, trustworthy product.

Some of the best financial products turn compliance from a burden into a feature.

Speed Versus Certainty

In regulated industries, the cost of getting it wrong is much higher than the cost of being slow. A compliance violation can result in fines, enforcement actions, or loss of license to operate. Moving fast is not worth that risk.

This means calibrating your expectations for development speed. Things will take longer than they would in an unregulated environment. That is not dysfunction; it is appropriate caution.

The goal is not speed. The goal is certainty. Being confident that what you ship is correct, compliant, and sustainable.

What Transfers to Other Industries

Not every industry is as heavily regulated as financial services. But the lessons apply more broadly than you might think:

Documentation discipline improves decision-making everywhere, not just in regulated contexts.

Stakeholder management matters whenever external parties have influence over your success.

Integrating constraints into experience rather than fighting them is a general product principle.

Choosing certainty over speed is appropriate whenever the cost of failure is high.

Building in a regulated industry forces you to develop these disciplines. Once you have them, they make you better at building anywhere.

From Idea to Product: The OfferPath Story

By Calvin Williams Jr. | February 6, 2025 | 8 min read

Building a product from scratch teaches you things that working on existing products never can. With OfferPath, I experienced the full journey from initial idea through development, launch, and iteration. Here is what I learned.

Where It Started

OfferPath began with a problem I saw repeatedly: people struggling with the complexity of job offer evaluation and negotiation. The information existed, scattered across the internet and locked in individual experiences. But there was no good way to bring it together into actionable guidance for someone facing a specific offer.

The first question was whether this was actually a problem worth solving. Not every problem is. I talked to people, understood their pain points, and validated that yes, this was something that mattered to them. They were making important decisions with inadequate information and support.

The Build Decision

Even with a validated problem, the question remained: should I build a product to solve it?

This is where my own advice about when not to build came back to me. I had to honestly evaluate whether existing solutions were adequate, whether the market was large enough, whether I could build something meaningfully better.

The analysis said yes. Existing resources were fragmented and generic. The market was substantial and growing. And I had specific ideas about how to make the experience better.

So I started building.

Early Mistakes

I made mistakes that, in retrospect, I should have known to avoid. I built too much before getting user feedback. I added features based on my assumptions rather than validated needs. I spent time on polish when I should have spent time on fundamentals.

These are common first-time builder mistakes. Knowing about them intellectually does not fully protect you from making them. But each mistake was a lesson that improved subsequent decisions.

What Worked

Talking to Users Early and Often

The features that worked best were the ones shaped by real user feedback. Every conversation with someone using the product, or trying to use it, taught me something I would not have figured out on my own.

This feedback loop is not optional. It is the core of good product development. Without it, you are building based on assumptions that may or may not be true.

Starting Simple

The initial version of OfferPath was much simpler than what I originally envisioned. This was not a compromise; it was the right approach. A simple version could ship sooner, get feedback sooner, and iterate sooner.

Every feature you add is a feature you have to maintain, explain, and support. Starting simple keeps the burden manageable while you are still learning what users actually need.

Measuring What Matters

I defined success metrics before launch and tracked them rigorously. Not vanity metrics about traffic or sign-ups, but metrics that connected to the core value proposition: were users actually getting help with their offer decisions?

These metrics were sometimes uncomfortable to look at. But they were honest, and they guided iteration toward what actually mattered.

The Ongoing Journey

A product is never done. Every launch is just a milestone in an ongoing journey of improvement. What users need evolves. What competitors offer evolves. What is technically possible evolves.

The mindset shift from "building a product" to "continuously improving a product" is significant. It requires patience, commitment, and genuine interest in serving users over the long term.

What I Would Tell Past Me

If I could go back and give myself advice before starting OfferPath, it would be this:

  • Talk to more users sooner. There is no substitute for real feedback from real people.
  • Launch before you feel ready. If you are comfortable with the launch, you probably waited too long.
  • Focus on the core value proposition. Everything else can wait.
  • Measure honestly. Vanity metrics feel good but teach nothing.
  • Be patient. Good products take time to find their audience and refine their offering.

Why This Matters for You

I share this not because OfferPath is remarkable, but because the lessons are transferable. Whether you are building your first product or your twentieth, the fundamentals are the same: understand the problem, validate before building, start simple, get feedback early, measure honestly, and iterate continuously.

These are not secrets. But knowing them and doing them are different things. The gap between knowledge and execution is where most products fail.

The Exit Planning Question Every Business Owner Ignores

By Calvin Williams Jr. | February 13, 2025 | 6 min read

You will leave your business someday. Every owner does, one way or another. The question is whether you will leave on your terms or whether circumstances will decide for you.

After advising business owners for years, many of them in their fifties and sixties, I have noticed a peculiar pattern: the owners who built the most valuable, transferable companies were the ones who started thinking about exit years before they planned to leave.

And the ones who struggled? They avoided the question until it was too late to do anything about it.

The Question Nobody Wants to Answer

Here it is: Could your business run for twelve months without you?

Not survive. Not limp along. Actually run. Hit its numbers. Serve its customers. Handle problems. Grow.

For most owners, the honest answer is no. And that answer has profound implications for the value of what you have built.

Why This Matters Now

A business that depends on its owner is not really a business in the way that buyers think about it. It is a job that happens to have business trappings. And jobs are not worth much to anyone other than the person holding them.

When you go to sell, or when you are forced to stop working due to health or circumstances, a buyer looks at your company and asks a simple question: will this thing keep generating money without the current owner?

If the answer is uncertain, the price goes down. Sometimes dramatically. Sometimes to zero.

The Five Dependencies That Destroy Value

1. Customer Relationships That Live in Your Head

If you are the one who knows the customers, who has the relationships, who gets the calls when there is a problem, you have a dependency. When you leave, those relationships go with you.

The fix is systematic: documented customer information, multiple points of contact, formal handoff procedures, and deliberate relationship building by others in your organization.

2. Decisions That Only You Can Make

Every time someone in your company has to wait for you to decide something, you are bottlenecking the business. More importantly, you are demonstrating that the business lacks the judgment to function independently.

The fix is cultural: push decision-making down, accept that some decisions will be made differently than you would make them, and build capacity for independent judgment throughout the organization.

3. Expertise That Is Not Documented

You know things about how the business works that nobody else knows. Maybe it is technical expertise, maybe it is operational knowledge, maybe it is supplier relationships. Whatever it is, if it is in your head and not on paper, it disappears when you do.

The fix is tedious but essential: document everything. Create manuals. Record training videos. Build systems that capture institutional knowledge.

4. Financial Complexity That Requires Your Interpretation

If you are the only one who really understands the numbers, who knows why certain expenses exist, who can explain the business model to a skeptical analyst, you have a problem.

The fix is transparency: clean financials, clear reporting, and people other than you who can explain what is happening and why.

5. Growth That Depends on Your Energy

Some owners are the drivers of all new business development. They are the rainmakers, the networkers, the ones who bring in the opportunities. When they step back, growth stops.

The fix is structural: build systems that generate opportunities regardless of owner involvement. This might be the hardest dependency to break, but it is essential for transferable value.

The Three-Year Timeline

These dependencies cannot be fixed overnight. Building a business that runs without you is a project that takes years, not months. Which is why the time to start is now, regardless of when you think you might want to exit.

A realistic timeline looks something like this:

Year one: Document everything. Identify all owner dependencies. Start building systems and training people to take over your functions.

Year two: Step back deliberately. Stop doing things you have always done and see what breaks. Fix what breaks. Repeat.

Year three: Run extended tests where you are uninvolved for weeks at a time. Verify that the business performs. Make adjustments.

At the end of this process, you have something genuinely valuable: a business that a buyer can acquire with confidence that it will continue performing.

"The best time to start exit planning was ten years ago. The second best time is today."

This Is Also About Quality of Life

I have framed this in terms of exit value, but there is another benefit to building a business that can run without you: you get your life back.

The owner who is chained to their business, who cannot take a vacation without constant calls, who cannot get sick without everything falling apart, is not free. They own a job, not a business.

Building toward owner-independence gives you options you do not currently have. Maybe you want to exit in five years. Maybe you want to stay involved for twenty more. Either way, you want the choice to be yours.

Why "Trust Me" Doesn't Work Anymore (And What Does)

By Calvin Williams Jr. | February 20, 2025 | 6 min read

There was a time when professional credentials were enough. You hung your degree on the wall, put years in the business, and people trusted that you knew what you were doing. That time is over.

The business owners I work with are skeptical, and they should be. They have been burned by smooth talkers who promised results and delivered excuses. They have hired expensive consultants who created elaborate decks and left without changing anything. They have invested in initiatives that generated reports but not results.

Trust has to be earned differently now. Here is how.

The Erosion of Assumed Trust

Three forces have combined to make "trust me" obsolete:

Information abundance. Your potential clients can research you, your competitors, and your industry in ways that were impossible twenty years ago. They come to conversations knowing more than ever before. Claims that once went unquestioned now get verified.

Increased competition. In most markets, there are more options than ever before. If a client is skeptical of you, they have alternatives. The power has shifted toward buyers who can demand proof before committing.

Accumulated disappointment. Business owners have been let down enough times that they have developed antibodies. The same pitches that worked on them five years ago now trigger resistance. They have heard the promises before and seen them fail.

What Builds Trust Now

Demonstrated Competence Over Claimed Expertise

Telling someone you are an expert means nothing. Showing them means everything.

This is why sharing your thinking publicly, whether through content or conversations, builds trust so effectively. When you publish detailed analysis that demonstrates deep understanding, you are proving expertise rather than claiming it. When you share specific examples with lessons learned, you are showing rather than telling.

The key is substance. Generic content that anyone could produce does not demonstrate anything. Specific, detailed, genuinely useful insights that require real expertise to generate build credibility in a way that credentials cannot.

Transparency About Limitations

Counterintuitively, being clear about what you cannot do or do not know builds more trust than pretending you can do everything.

When someone admits a limitation upfront, it signals that their claims in other areas are more likely to be genuine. When someone promises the moon, everything they say becomes suspect.

This applies to services, to projections, to timelines, to everything. Saying "I am not sure, but here is how we would figure it out" builds more trust than fabricating certainty you do not have.

Aligned Incentives

Show me how you get paid, and I will tell you what advice you are going to give. This is the cynical but often accurate assumption sophisticated buyers bring to every conversation.

Business structures that align your success with the client's success build trust that marketing cannot. Skin in the game. Clear metrics. Honest assessment of risks and potential outcomes.

At a minimum, transparency about incentives helps. Explaining how you make money and how that might influence your recommendations at least demonstrates awareness that the conflict exists.

Track Record With Specifics

References and examples still matter, but they need to be more specific than they used to be. Vague mentions of being "great to work with" carry little weight.

What builds trust is specificity: Here was the situation. Here is what we did. Here are the results. Here is what the client says about the experience.

Time and Consistency

Trust compounds over time. Every interaction is a deposit or a withdrawal. Consistent delivery, consistent communication, consistent presence gradually builds the credibility that allows for deeper engagement.

This is why trying to rush relationships often backfires. The client who is not ready to trust you fully after one meeting might be ready after a year of seeing you deliver on small commitments. Patience matters.

The Trust Paradox

Here is the paradox: the people who most deserve trust often have the hardest time claiming it. They are too focused on doing good work to spend time marketing themselves. They are too honest to make the exaggerated claims their competitors make.

Meanwhile, the best marketers are often the worst performers. They have resources to invest in perception while neglecting substance.

If you are doing good work but struggling to earn the trust you deserve, the answer is not to start making empty promises. The answer is to find ways to make your actual competence visible. Document your results. Share your thinking. Let your work speak in venues where potential clients can see it.

What We Chose

At ScaleUp Ventures, we built our practice around demonstrable value. We show our analysis before asking for commitments. We tie our recommendations to clear outcomes. We are honest about what we do not know.

We do this not because it is clever marketing, though it works as marketing, but because we believe it is the right way to operate. "Trust me" is not good enough anymore. "Let me show you" is the only thing that is.

How AI Implementation Actually Works in the Real World

By Calvin Williams Jr. | February 27, 2025 | 5 min read

There is a gap between the AI hype and AI reality. The headlines talk about transformation and disruption. The reality is usually messier, more incremental, and more valuable than the grand narratives suggest.

Having helped organizations actually implement AI, not just talk about it, I have learned some things about what works and what does not.

Start With the Problem, Not the Technology

The most common mistake in AI implementation is starting with the technology. Someone reads about a new capability, gets excited, and looks for ways to use it. This is backwards.

Successful AI implementation starts with a clear problem. What is costing you time? What is limiting your capacity? What decision do you make repeatedly that could be made better with more information?

Once you have a clear problem, you can evaluate whether AI is the right solution. Sometimes it is. Sometimes a simpler approach works better. But you cannot know until you understand the problem.

The Value Is Often Mundane

The splashy AI demonstrations involve creative writing, image generation, or complex reasoning. The valuable AI implementations often involve much more mundane tasks.

Summarizing documents. Extracting information from unstructured data. Drafting routine communications. Analyzing patterns in large datasets. Automating repetitive cognitive work.

These are not exciting. They will not get you on a conference stage. But they save real time and create real value for real organizations.

Human + AI Beats Either Alone

The most effective AI implementations are not full automation. They are augmentation. AI does what it does well, humans do what they do well, and the combination is better than either alone.

AI generates drafts; humans refine them. AI surfaces patterns; humans interpret them. AI handles volume; humans handle exceptions. This division of labor plays to the strengths of both.

Organizations that try to fully automate cognitive work usually either fail or create problems that require significant cleanup. Organizations that use AI to augment human capability get consistent wins.

The Adoption Challenge

Technical implementation is often the easy part. The hard part is getting people to actually use the tools you have built.

Change is difficult. People have established workflows. New tools require learning. Even beneficial changes face resistance.

Successful AI implementation requires attention to adoption from the beginning. Who will use this? What is their incentive? What barriers will they face? How will you support them through the transition?

Technology that nobody uses generates no value, no matter how sophisticated it is.

Start Small, Learn, Scale

Grand AI transformations usually fail. Modest AI pilots usually succeed, and then can be scaled.

Start with one use case. Prove it works. Learn what you did not anticipate. Adjust. Then expand.

This approach is slower than the big-bang transformation some leaders want. But it actually works, and the big-bang approaches usually do not.

The Questions That Matter

Before any AI implementation, answer these questions:

  • What specific problem are we solving? If you cannot articulate this clearly, stop.
  • Why is AI the right solution? What makes this problem suited for AI rather than simpler approaches?
  • How will we measure success? What specific outcomes indicate this is working?
  • Who will use this and why? What is the adoption plan?
  • What happens when it is wrong? AI makes mistakes. What is the error handling strategy?

If you have good answers to these questions, you are probably on the right track. If you do not, you are probably about to waste resources.

The Opportunity Is Real

Despite the hype and disappointment cycles, AI genuinely can create significant value for organizations. The technology is real. The capabilities are substantial. The question is whether you implement thoughtfully or chase trends.

Thoughtful implementation starts with problems, focuses on practical value, combines human and machine capabilities, attends to adoption, and scales from proven success. It is not glamorous, but it works.

The Patience Advantage: What a Heron Taught Us About Business Growth

By Calvin Williams Jr. | March 6, 2025 | 7 min read

If you have ever watched a great blue heron hunt, you have seen something remarkable. This bird stands motionless at the water's edge, sometimes for hours. It does not chase. It does not rush. It waits.

And then, in a fraction of a second, it strikes. The fish never sees it coming. The heron eats. It returns to stillness.

There is a lesson here for every business owner drowning in the noise of "move fast," "disrupt," and "hustle." The heron has survived for millions of years not despite its patience but because of it.

The Cult of Speed

We live in a business culture that celebrates velocity. First-mover advantage. Rapid iteration. Minimum viable products. Growth at all costs. The assumption is that faster is always better, that hesitation is weakness, that the winners are the ones who move first and figure it out later.

For some businesses, in some situations, this is true. Startups competing in winner-take-all markets may need to sacrifice caution for speed.

But most of us are not building venture-backed startups. Most of us run businesses where sustainability matters more than scale, where customers value reliability over novelty, where the goal is building something that lasts rather than something that exits.

For these businesses, patience is not a weakness. It is a competitive advantage.

What Patience Actually Means

Patience in business does not mean passivity. The heron is not lounging at the water's edge. It is intensely focused. Every sense is tuned to the water. It is ready to act the instant conditions align.

Business patience looks similar:

Waiting for the right opportunity. Not every lead is worth pursuing. Not every market is worth entering. Not every hire is worth making. Patience means having the discipline to say no to good opportunities while waiting for great ones.

Giving strategies time to work. New initiatives need time to mature. New hires need time to learn. Process improvements need time to compound. The impatient operator abandons approaches before they have had a fair chance to prove themselves.

Building relationships before asking for business. The best clients come from trust built over years. Trying to shortcut this process with aggressive sales tactics might generate quick wins, but it rarely builds lasting partnerships.

Developing people rather than replacing them. Talent development is slow. It is faster to fire and hire. But the organization that patiently develops its people ends up with deeper capability and stronger culture than the one that churns through staff.

The Math of Patience

Impatience has hidden costs that rarely appear on financial statements:

Opportunity cost of premature action. Every resource committed to a mediocre opportunity is a resource unavailable for the excellent opportunity that comes later. Patience preserves options.

Relationship damage from rushing. Pushing too hard, too fast damages trust that takes years to rebuild. The deal you force closed today may cost you five deals from that client over the next decade.

Learning lost to rapid pivoting. When you abandon an approach before it has played out, you do not know whether it failed or whether you just did not give it enough time. This lack of learning makes future decisions worse.

Team burnout from constant urgency. Organizations that treat everything as urgent eventually cannot distinguish real emergencies from manufactured ones. The team becomes exhausted and cynical.

When Patience Breaks

Patience is not infinite. Even the heron eventually moves to a different spot if no fish appear. The question is when patience becomes passive and inaction becomes avoidance.

The heron's patience works because it is backed by clear criteria: is this water likely to hold fish? Is this a good hunting position? Am I seeing any signs of activity?

Business patience should work the same way. You are not waiting indefinitely. You are waiting while specific conditions are or are not being met:

  • This initiative needs six months to produce meaningful data. Have we given it six months?
  • This new hire typically takes a year to reach full productivity. Are we at a year?
  • This market expansion requires reaching certain milestones. Are we trending toward those milestones?

When the criteria are met but results are not appearing, patience becomes stubbornness. The heron moves to new water.

The Certainty Connection

At ScaleUp Ventures, we adopted the great blue heron as our symbol because it represents what we believe about business growth. We stand for certainty first, which means doing the analysis before taking action, understanding the math before spending the money, having a clear thesis before making the bet.

This approach requires patience. You cannot rush analysis. You cannot shortcut understanding. You cannot manufacture certainty through activity.

But when the work is done and the opportunity is right, we move decisively. Like the heron's strike, effective business action is often fast when it finally happens. The patience that precedes it is what makes the speed productive rather than reckless.

A Different Kind of Confidence

There is a particular confidence that comes from patience. It is not the brash certainty of youth, which does not know enough to be humble. It is the earned assurance of experience, which has seen enough cycles to know that most urgency is manufactured and most opportunities are not as time-sensitive as they appear.

The impatient competitor chases every trend, reacts to every move, exhausts themselves running in circles. The patient competitor watches, learns, waits, and then moves with precision when the moment is right.

Over a career, over a business lifetime, which approach wins? The heron has been answering that question for 1.8 million years. We have taken notice.

"Certainty first. Then decisive action. This is the patience advantage."
