Marketing & Tech Terms in Plain English

A no-nonsense glossary for founders and business owners who want straight answers, not consultant-speak.

If you have ever sat in a meeting and nodded along while wondering what the agency was actually talking about, this guide is for you. No jargon. No fluff. Just clear explanations of what these terms mean and whether they matter for your business.

Money & Returns

Terms that relate to what you spend and what you get back

Term

ROI (Return on Investment)

What It Actually Means

How much money you made compared to how much you spent. If you spend $1,000 on marketing and it brings in $3,000 in new business, your ROI is 200% (you got back double what you put in, plus your original investment).

Why It Matters

This is the bottom line question: Is this marketing worth doing? Any marketing partner should be able to show you the ROI of their work, not just vanity metrics like "impressions" or "engagement."

Example

Ask your agency: "What ROI have you delivered for similar clients? I want to see the actual numbers, not just percentages."
Term

Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)

What It Actually Means

The total cost to get one new customer. Add up everything you spend on marketing and sales, then divide by how many new customers you got. If you spent $5,000 last month and got 10 new customers, your CAC is $500.

Why It Matters

You need to know this number to understand if your marketing is sustainable. If it costs you $500 to get a customer who only spends $300, you are losing money on every sale.

Example

Reality check: "Our Google Ads are bringing in leads at $50 each, but only 1 in 10 becomes a customer. Our real CAC is $500, not $50."
Term

Lifetime Value (LTV or CLV)

What It Actually Means

How much money a typical customer spends with you over the entire time they do business with you. A customer who buys $200 worth of services every month for 3 years has a lifetime value of $7,200.

Why It Matters

This tells you how much you can afford to spend to get a new customer. If your average customer is worth $7,200 over time, spending $500 to get them is a good deal. This is why some businesses can afford expensive marketing that seems crazy at first glance.

Example

The calculation: "Our average customer stays 4 years and spends $150/month. That's $7,200 LTV. We can afford to spend up to $720 to acquire them (10% of LTV is a common benchmark)."

Website Terms

Making your website work harder for your business

Term

SEO (Search Engine Optimization)

What It Actually Means

Making changes to your website so it shows up when people search on Google. This includes writing content that matches what people are searching for, making your site load quickly, and getting other reputable websites to link to yours.

Why It Matters

When someone searches "plumber near me" or "best insurance agent in Dallas," you want your business to show up, not your competitors. SEO is about earning that visibility without paying for each click.

Example

What good SEO looks like: "We optimized your service pages for 'commercial HVAC repair Dallas' and after 4 months, you now appear on page 1. This brought in 12 new leads last month without ad spend."
Term

Landing Page

What It Actually Means

A single webpage designed with one specific goal: to get visitors to take one action (call you, fill out a form, request a quote). Unlike your regular website pages that have menus and multiple options, a landing page removes distractions and focuses on conversion.

Why It Matters

When you pay for advertising, you want every visitor to have the best chance of becoming a customer. Sending ad traffic to your homepage (with its many distractions) wastes money. A focused landing page can double or triple your results from the same ad spend.

Example

Before/After: "We sent Google Ads traffic to your homepage: 2% became leads. Same traffic to a focused landing page: 8% became leads. Same ad budget, 4x the results."
Term

Conversion Rate

What It Actually Means

The percentage of website visitors who do what you want them to do (call, fill out a form, make a purchase). If 100 people visit your site and 5 request a quote, your conversion rate is 5%.

Why It Matters

This is one of the most important numbers in marketing. Improving your conversion rate from 2% to 4% doubles your leads without spending an extra dollar on advertising. Often, fixing your conversion rate is cheaper and faster than buying more traffic.

Example

The math: "You get 1,000 visitors/month and convert 2% (20 leads). If we improve conversion to 4%, you get 40 leads from the same traffic. That's like doubling your ad budget for free."
Term

CTA (Call to Action)

What It Actually Means

The button, link, or instruction that tells visitors what to do next. "Call Now," "Get Your Free Quote," "Schedule a Consultation" are all calls to action. It is the specific next step you want someone to take.

Why It Matters

People need clear direction. A website without obvious CTAs is like a store with no checkout counter. You might have great products, but if people do not know how to buy, they leave empty-handed.

Example

Good vs. weak: Weak: "Contact Us" / Strong: "Get Your Free Roof Inspection - We'll Be There Tomorrow"
Term

A/B Testing

What It Actually Means

Showing two different versions of something (a webpage, email, or ad) to different groups of people to see which one works better. Version A might have a blue button, Version B has a green button. You see which gets more clicks, then use the winner.

Why It Matters

This removes guessing from marketing decisions. Instead of debating whether your homepage headline should say X or Y, you test both and let real customers tell you which works. Small improvements compound over time.

Example

Real test: "We tested two headlines. 'Quality Service Since 1985' got a 3% conversion rate. 'Same-Day Service, Guaranteed' got 7%. The second headline now runs on all ads."
Term

Organic Traffic

What It Actually Means

Visitors who find your website through unpaid search results. They searched for something on Google, saw your site in the results, and clicked through without you paying for that click.

Why It Matters

Organic traffic is "free" in the sense that you do not pay per click. It takes time and effort to build (that is what SEO is for), but once you rank well, you get steady traffic without ongoing ad costs. It is like owning vs. renting.

Example

The difference: "Paid traffic: You pay $3 per click, traffic stops when budget runs out. Organic traffic: Free clicks, keeps coming even when you are on vacation."
Term

Paid Traffic

What It Actually Means

Visitors who come to your website through ads you paid for, whether on Google, Facebook, or other platforms. You pay for each click or each thousand views.

Why It Matters

Paid traffic gives you immediate results and precise control. You can turn it on, off, up, or down instantly. The trade-off is cost. Most businesses need a mix of paid (for quick results) and organic (for long-term sustainability).

Example

When to use it: "We use paid traffic to test new markets quickly. Once we know what works, we build organic content around those topics for long-term traffic."

Advertising Terms

Understanding what you are paying for when you run ads

Term

PPC (Pay-Per-Click)

What It Actually Means

Advertising where you only pay when someone actually clicks on your ad. Google Ads is the most common example. If your ad shows 1,000 times but only 50 people click, you only pay for those 50 clicks.

Why It Matters

You pay for actual interest, not just eyeballs. This makes PPC very measurable. You can calculate exactly what each lead costs you. The downside: competitive keywords can get expensive, especially in industries like law, insurance, and home services.

Example

Budget reality: "'Emergency plumber' costs $45 per click. At a 5% conversion rate, each lead costs $900. Make sure that math works for your business before committing."
Term

Retargeting (also called Remarketing)

What It Actually Means

Showing ads to people who already visited your website. Ever looked at a product on Amazon and then seen ads for it everywhere? That is retargeting. A small piece of code on your site tracks visitors, and you can show them ads later.

Why It Matters

Most people do not buy on their first visit. Retargeting keeps your business in front of people who already showed interest. These "warm" audiences typically convert much better than cold audiences, making retargeting very cost-effective.

Example

How it works: "Someone visits your 'commercial cleaning' page but does not call. For the next 30 days, they see your ads on other websites reminding them of your service."
Term

Impressions

What It Actually Means

The number of times your ad was displayed. One person seeing your ad five times counts as five impressions. This measures visibility, not engagement.

Why It Matters

Impressions are a "top of funnel" metric. High impressions with low clicks means your ad is not compelling. Be careful when agencies report impressive impression numbers. Impressions do not pay your bills. Customers do.

Example

Watch out: "Your ad got 100,000 impressions!" sounds great until you realize only 100 people clicked and 2 became customers. Focus on the numbers that matter.
Term

Click-Through Rate (CTR)

What It Actually Means

The percentage of people who saw your ad and clicked on it. If 1,000 people see your ad and 20 click, your CTR is 2%. This measures how compelling your ad is.

Why It Matters

A higher CTR usually means lower costs in platforms like Google Ads (they reward relevant ads). It also tells you if your message resonates. Low CTR means your headline, image, or offer needs work.

Example

Benchmark: "Average Google Ads CTR is 2-3%. If yours is below 1%, your ad copy needs work. Above 5% and you are doing something right."

Finding Customers

Terms about attracting and converting potential buyers

Term

Lead Generation

What It Actually Means

Any marketing activity designed to get potential customers to identify themselves and express interest. When someone fills out your contact form, calls your number, or downloads your guide, they become a "lead." Lead generation is the process of making that happen.

Why It Matters

Without leads, you have no one to sell to. Lead generation is often the primary job of marketing. Everything else, the website, the ads, the content, exists to generate leads for your sales team (or for you) to close.

Example

Simple version: "Our lead generation campaign includes Google Ads driving to a landing page with a 'Free Consultation' offer. Goal: 50 qualified leads per month."
Term

Funnel

What It Actually Means

The journey customers take from first hearing about you to actually buying. It is called a funnel because many people enter at the top (awareness), but fewer come out the bottom (purchase). Each stage filters some people out.

Why It Matters

Understanding your funnel helps you find problems. If lots of people visit your site but few contact you, the problem is your website. If lots contact you but few buy, the problem is your sales process. Different problems need different solutions.

Example

A typical funnel: "1,000 see your ad, 100 click to your site, 10 fill out your form, 3 show up for consultations, 2 become customers. Now you know where to focus."
Term

CRM (Customer Relationship Management)

What It Actually Means

Software that keeps track of all your customer and prospect information in one place. Contact info, communication history, what they bought, follow-up reminders. Think of it as a very sophisticated address book combined with a to-do list for sales.

Why It Matters

As your business grows, you cannot keep track of everything in your head or on sticky notes. A CRM ensures leads do not fall through cracks and helps you follow up consistently. Most agencies will want you to have one to track the leads they send you.

Example

Common options: "HubSpot, Salesforce, and Zoho are popular CRMs. For smaller businesses, even a simple tool like Pipedrive or a spreadsheet is better than nothing."
Term

Qualified Lead

What It Actually Means

A potential customer who actually fits your business and is likely to buy. Not everyone who contacts you is a good fit. A "qualified" lead has the budget, the need, and the decision-making power to become a customer.

Why It Matters

Ten qualified leads beat 100 unqualified ones. When evaluating marketing, look at lead quality, not just quantity. An agency sending you tire-kickers and price-shoppers is not helping, even if the numbers look impressive.

Example

Define it clearly: "For us, a qualified lead is a business with 20+ employees, in our service area, with budget over $5,000/month, and decision-maker engaged."

Content & Marketing Strategy

Different approaches to reaching and persuading customers

Term

Content Marketing

What It Actually Means

Creating useful content (articles, videos, guides) that attracts potential customers to your business. Instead of interrupting people with ads, you provide something valuable that draws them in. Blog posts, how-to videos, and industry guides are common examples.

Why It Matters

Content marketing builds trust and positions you as an expert before someone ever talks to you. It also helps with SEO (search engines love useful content). The trade-off: it takes time. Do not expect immediate results like you get from ads.

Example

In practice: "An HVAC company writes '10 Signs Your AC Needs Replacement.' People searching this topic find the article, see the company as knowledgeable, and call them when ready to buy."
Term

Inbound Marketing

What It Actually Means

Marketing that draws customers to you rather than you going out to find them. SEO, content marketing, and social media are inbound tactics. The idea is to be helpful and visible so potential customers find you when they need you.

Why It Matters

Inbound leads often close better because the customer came to you. They already have a need and are actively looking. Compare this to cold calling (outbound), where you interrupt people who may not be interested.

Example

The contrast: "Outbound: You call 100 businesses hoping 5 need your service. Inbound: 5 businesses search 'commercial cleaning Dallas,' find your article, and call you."
Term

Email Marketing

What It Actually Means

Using email to stay in touch with prospects and customers. This includes newsletters, promotional emails, and automated follow-up sequences. You build a list of email addresses and communicate with them regularly.

Why It Matters

Email is still one of the highest-ROI marketing channels. You own your email list (unlike social media followers). Regular, valuable emails keep you top of mind so when customers are ready to buy, they think of you first.

Example

Simple approach: "Monthly newsletter with helpful tips and one offer. Does not need to be fancy. Consistency matters more than perfection."
Term

Lead Magnet

What It Actually Means

Something valuable you give away for free in exchange for someone's contact information. A free guide, checklist, calculator, or consultation. The "magnet" attracts leads to give you their email or phone number.

Why It Matters

People are protective of their contact info. They will not give it up for nothing. A lead magnet offers a fair trade: something useful for them, contact info for you. The better your lead magnet, the more leads you capture.

Example

Good examples: "Free 15-minute consultation," "Commercial Insurance Checklist," "2024 Tax Deduction Guide for Small Businesses."

Measuring Results

How to know if your marketing is working

Term

KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)

What It Actually Means

The specific numbers you track to measure success. KPIs are the metrics that actually matter for your goals. If your goal is more sales, KPIs might be leads generated, conversion rate, and revenue from new customers.

Why It Matters

You can measure a thousand things, but not all of them matter. KPIs focus attention on what counts. When an agency says they improved your "engagement," ask: did that translate to sales? If not, it is not a real KPI for your business.

Example

Agree upfront: "Before we start, let's agree on 3-4 KPIs. I care about: leads per month, cost per lead, lead-to-customer rate, and revenue from marketing."
Term

Metrics

What It Actually Means

Any number you can measure about your marketing. Website visitors, email opens, social media followers, ad clicks. Metrics are the raw data. KPIs are the metrics that actually matter for your business goals.

Why It Matters

More data is not always better. Drowning in metrics can distract you from what matters. Be suspicious of reports with dozens of metrics but no clear connection to revenue or leads. Ask: "What does this number mean for my bottom line?"

Example

Cut through the noise: "I do not need to know my 'bounce rate' or 'pages per session.' Just tell me: How many leads came in? What did they cost? How many became customers?"
Term

Analytics

What It Actually Means

The process of collecting and analyzing data about your marketing performance. Also refers to the tools used to do this (like Google Analytics). Analytics tells you what is happening on your website, where visitors come from, and what they do.

Why It Matters

You cannot improve what you do not measure. Analytics lets you make decisions based on facts, not guesses. It reveals what is working (do more of it) and what is not (fix or stop it). Any serious marketing effort needs basic analytics in place.

Example

Basic setup: "At minimum, you need Google Analytics on your site and call tracking on your phone number. Otherwise, you're flying blind."
Term

Attribution

What It Actually Means

Figuring out which marketing activity deserves credit for a sale. If a customer saw your ad, then visited your website, then got an email, then called you, which of those touchpoints made the sale happen? Attribution tries to answer that question.

Why It Matters

Without attribution, you do not know where to spend your marketing budget. You might think your Facebook ads are not working, when actually they introduce people who later convert through Google. Attribution helps you see the full picture.

Example

The challenge: "Ask every lead: 'How did you hear about us?' It's not perfect, but it's a start. For digital, use UTM tracking codes on your links."

Technology Terms

The tech behind modern marketing (without the hype)

Term

Automation

What It Actually Means

Using software to do repetitive tasks automatically. In marketing, this usually means automatic email follow-ups, scheduling social posts in advance, or automatically routing leads to the right salesperson.

Why It Matters

Automation saves time and ensures consistency. A new lead gets a follow-up email immediately, even at 2 AM. But automation is only as good as your strategy. Automating a bad process just makes you fail faster.

Example

Simple automation: "When someone fills out your contact form, they automatically get a 'Thank you' email with next steps, and you get a notification on your phone."
Term

AI (Artificial Intelligence)

What It Actually Means

Software that can learn from data and make decisions or predictions. In marketing, AI is used for things like predicting which leads are most likely to buy, personalizing website content, or writing first drafts of content.

Why It Matters

AI is powerful but often oversold. Many "AI-powered" tools are just fancy automation with a trendy label. Real AI can improve efficiency, but it is a tool, not magic. Focus on results, not buzzwords.

Example

Practical AI: "AI can analyze which of your leads are most likely to close based on patterns in your data. This helps you prioritize follow-up on the best opportunities."
Term

Machine Learning

What It Actually Means

A type of AI where software improves automatically by learning from data. The more data it sees, the better it gets. Google's ad system uses machine learning to show your ads to people most likely to click based on patterns it has learned.

Why It Matters

Machine learning powers many marketing tools you already use. Google and Facebook use it to optimize your ad delivery. The main thing to know: these systems get better with more data, so do not expect immediate perfection.

Example

In practice: "Google's 'Smart Bidding' uses machine learning to adjust your bids automatically. It needs about 30 conversions per month to work well."
Term

Integration

What It Actually Means

Connecting different software tools so they share data automatically. For example, connecting your website forms to your CRM so new leads appear automatically, or connecting your CRM to your email tool so you can send targeted campaigns.

Why It Matters

Without integration, you waste time copying data between systems and increase the chance of errors. Good integrations mean less manual work and better data. When evaluating tools, always ask: "Does this integrate with what I already use?"

Example

Key question: "If my website form, CRM, and email tool do not talk to each other, leads fall through cracks. Make sure your systems are connected."

Brand Building

Building long-term recognition and trust

Term

Brand Awareness

What It Actually Means

How many people know your business exists and what you do. High brand awareness means when people need your type of service, your name comes to mind. Low brand awareness means you are invisible to potential customers.

Why It Matters

People buy from businesses they recognize and trust. Brand awareness creates a foundation that makes all your other marketing more effective. That said, awareness alone does not pay bills. It needs to translate into leads and sales.

Example

The goal: "When someone in your area thinks 'I need a commercial cleaning company,' your name should be one of the first that comes to mind."
Term

Brand Equity

What It Actually Means

The value that comes from people having positive feelings about your brand. A business with strong brand equity can charge more, win deals more easily, and attract better employees. It is the premium people pay to work with a trusted name.

Why It Matters

Brand equity is a long-term asset. It takes years to build and can be destroyed quickly. Every customer interaction either builds or erodes it. When you think about marketing, think about what each action does for your brand equity, not just immediate sales.

Example

Real value: "Why can some plumbers charge $150/hour while others struggle at $75? Brand equity. Customers pay more for a name they trust."
Term

Positioning

What It Actually Means

How you want customers to think about your business compared to competitors. Are you the cheapest? The fastest? The most reliable? The premium option? Positioning is the specific place you want to occupy in people's minds.

Why It Matters

You cannot be everything to everyone. Clear positioning helps the right customers find you and helps you stand out from competitors. Unclear positioning means you compete on price alone, which is a race to the bottom.

Example

Define it: "We're not the cheapest insurance agency. We're the one that actually returns your calls and fights for your claims. That's our position."
Term

Value Proposition

What It Actually Means

A clear statement of what you offer, who it is for, and why they should choose you over alternatives. It answers the customer's question: "Why should I buy from you?" in a simple, compelling way.

Why It Matters

If you cannot clearly explain your value, customers will not understand it either. A strong value proposition makes all your marketing more effective because it gives people a reason to pay attention. Weak value propositions lead to price shopping.

Example

Template: "We help [specific customers] achieve [specific result] through [your approach], unlike [alternatives] that [limitation]."
