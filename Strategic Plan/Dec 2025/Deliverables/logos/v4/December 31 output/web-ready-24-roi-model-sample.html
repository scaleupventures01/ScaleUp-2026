ROI Modeling Framework

How We Calculate Whether Custom Software Makes Financial Sense

Deliverable Type Discovery Phase Analysis
Purpose Go/No-Go Decision Support
Output Build, Buy, or Don't Proceed

1 Why ROI Modeling Matters

The Uncomfortable Truth

Most software projects fail to deliver positive ROI. Not because of technical failures, but because nobody did the math first. Companies spend six figures on software that saves them five figures, or build custom solutions when a SaaS tool would work fine.

Our ROI model exists to answer one question before any code is written: Should you actually do this?

Key Principle: We get paid the same whether you proceed or not. This removes our incentive to inflate projections. If the numbers don't work, we'll tell you, and we have.

2 The ROI Framework

Every ROI model we build follows this structure. The specific numbers come from your business; the framework ensures we capture all relevant factors.

Component What We Measure
Current State Costs Labor hours, error rates, opportunity cost, tool subscriptions
Build Investment Development cost, implementation time, training, maintenance
Projected Savings Time saved, errors prevented, capacity unlocked
Revenue Impact New capabilities, faster delivery, competitive advantage
Payback Period Months until investment is recovered
3-Year ROI Total return over useful life of the software

How We Use the Scenarios

The decision is based on the Conservative scenario, not the Expected one. If the Conservative scenario shows negative or marginal ROI, we recommend against proceeding, even if Expected and Optimistic look great.

This protects you from optimism bias, which kills more software projects than technical debt.

4 When the Answer is "Don't Build"

Real Example: The No-Build Decision

During a discovery engagement, our ROI model revealed that a proposed internal tool would cost more to build and maintain than the manual process it was meant to replace. The client was surprised; they had assumed automation always saves money.

The analysis showed:

  • The manual process took approximately 20 hours/month across the team
  • Custom software would require significant upfront investment plus ongoing maintenance
  • An existing SaaS tool could handle 80% of the need at a fraction of the cost
  • The remaining 20% wasn't painful enough to justify the delta
Outcome
Client saved significant capital by not building. They used the SaaS tool instead.

Why this matters: Any vendor who makes money on builds has an incentive to recommend building. Our discovery model separates the analysis from the build, so you get honest recommendations.
