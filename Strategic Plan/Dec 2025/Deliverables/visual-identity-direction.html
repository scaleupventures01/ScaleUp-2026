Section 01

Overall Visual Concept

Creative Direction
Trusted Authority with Measured Ambition
The ScaleUp Ventures visual identity should project the confidence of a seasoned advisor combined with the forward-thinking perspective of a growth partner. The aesthetic balances institutional gravitas with approachable warmth - signaling to business owners aged 30-55 that we understand their world while bringing modern capabilities they need but cannot access themselves. This is not a startup aesthetic; it is a private equity-adjacent visual language that commands respect and builds immediate trust.
Strategic Rationale
Why this direction: Our validated personas (Staffing Agencies, Veterinary Practices, Pest Control, Trades) share key characteristics: owners aged 30-55, seeking certainty before (48% leave due to poor delivery), skeptical of "sales reps" (only 4% trust), and value trust over cost (80% of SME leaders). The visual identity must immediately signal credibility, accountability, and a peer-to-peer relationship - not vendor-to-client. The ROI-focused differentiator ("We build a model showing how you make your money back before you spend a dollar") demands visual proof of business sophistication.
What This Visual Identity IS
  • Confident without being aggressive
  • Sophisticated without being intimidating
  • Modern without being trendy
  • Warm without being casual
  • Established without being dated
  • Premium without being elitist
What This Visual Identity IS NOT
  • Startup-y or tech-bro aesthetic
  • Overly colorful or playful
  • Generic agency template look
  • Cold, sterile, or corporate
  • Trendy gradients or glassmorphism
  • Cheap or discount-signaling

Fonts to Avoid

Font Personalities to Avoid
  • Overly geometric sans-serifs (Futura, Avant Garde) - too cold and impersonal
  • Playful or quirky fonts (Comic Sans, Marker Felt) - undermines authority
  • Ultra-modern display fonts - signals "startup" to our 50+ audience
  • Script or handwritten fonts - too casual for business services
  • Condensed or narrow fonts - reduces readability for older eyes
  • Thin/light weights as primary text - accessibility concerns
Section 05

Imagery Style

Photography Direction
  • Subjects: Real business owners (40-60 age range), actual workplaces, genuine client interactions
  • Lighting: Natural, warm lighting with soft shadows - never harsh or clinical
  • Composition: Confident but approachable subjects, eye-level or slightly below shots
  • Environment: Professional settings - offices, meeting rooms, client businesses
  • Mood: Focused, determined, successful but not flashy
  • Color treatment: Slightly warm, desaturated for sophistication - no heavy filters
  • What to show: Collaboration, results discussions, real work being done
Photography to Avoid
  • Generic stock photos of handshakes or suits walking
  • Overly young/hipster tech startup imagery
  • People looking at phones or laptops with fake smiles
  • Abstract technology imagery (circuits, code screens)
  • Heavily filtered or Instagram-style aesthetics
  • Empty "aspirational lifestyle" imagery
  • Group high-fives or forced celebration shots
Illustration Approach
  • Style: Clean line work, minimal detail, purposeful communication
  • When to use: Concept explanation, process diagrams, abstract ideas
  • Color palette: Navy, gold, and warm neutrals only
  • Complexity: Simple enough to read at a glance, sophisticated enough to feel premium
  • Character: No cartoon people - use abstract representations or icons
  • Charts/data viz: Clean, professional, with gold highlights for key data
Iconography Style
  • Line weight: 2px consistent stroke weight
  • Style: Rounded corners, friendly but professional
  • Grid: 24px base grid, scalable
  • Color: Single color (navy or gold) - no multicolor icons
  • Complexity: Simple, recognizable at small sizes
  • Source: Custom or curated set (Phosphor, Lucide) - consistent family
Imagery Psychology
Our personas (average age 52-60) need to see themselves reflected in our imagery. Stock photos of 20-somethings in hoodies alienate our audience and signal "we don't understand your world." Photography featuring peers in recognizable business settings builds immediate affinity. The warm, natural lighting approach creates approachability without sacrificing professionalism - critical for owners who have been "seeking certainty before."
Section 06

Design Principles

01

Clarity Before Creativity

Every design decision serves communication first. Our audience values directness over decoration. If a visual element doesn't help convey the message, it doesn't belong. This means generous white space, clear hierarchy, and information that's easy to scan. Think: annual report quality, not agency portfolio flash.

02

Trust Through Consistency

Consistent application of colors, typography, and visual elements builds subconscious trust. No surprises, no gimmicks, no "creative" one-offs. Every touchpoint should feel like it comes from the same reliable source. Consistency signals: "We do what we say, every time."

03

Show The Work

Visualize processes, data, and results wherever possible. Our ROI-focused positioning demands visual proof. Charts, frameworks, before/after comparisons, and progress indicators should be design elements, not afterthoughts. Make accountability visible.

04

Respect The Reader

Design for our actual audience: business owners 50+ who are decision-makers, not design critics. This means readable text sizes (minimum 16px body), sufficient contrast, and accessible color combinations. Respect their time with scannable layouts. No hover-only information.

05

Premium, Not Precious

Quality materials and refined aesthetics signal value without being unapproachable. This isn't luxury branding - it's business-grade premium. Think: well-tailored suit, not couture fashion. The goal is respect and confidence, not intimidation or exclusivity.

Section 08

Logo Direction

Logo Concept
Growth With Foundation
The ScaleUp Ventures logo should communicate upward momentum anchored by stability. This is not a rocket ship startup - it's measured, sustainable growth built on a solid foundation. The visual should work at small sizes (favicons, app icons) and large formats (presentation slides, signage) while maintaining clarity and impact.
Concept Direction A: Abstract Growth Mark
A simple geometric mark suggesting upward trajectory - perhaps chevrons, ascending bars, or an abstract "S" that implies growth. Clean, modern, works well at all sizes. The mark should feel like it could belong to a financial institution or consultancy.
Concept Direction B: Refined Wordmark
A custom typography treatment of "ScaleUp" with "Ventures" in a lighter weight. The wordmark approach emphasizes the name recognition and works well for a company building trust through personal relationships. Consider subtle typographic customization.
Concept Direction C: Mark + Wordmark Lockup
A compact symbol paired with the name - versatile for various applications. The mark can stand alone for social icons and favicons while the full lockup appears on formal materials. This provides the most flexibility.
Concept Direction D: Monogram
A refined "SV" or "SU" monogram with subtle details. This approach carries the gravitas of established firms while creating a distinctive mark. Works well for business cards, letterheads, and professional applications.

Logo Requirements

Logo Must
  • Work in single color (navy or white)
  • Be legible at 24px height (favicon size)
  • Maintain clarity on dark and light backgrounds
  • Feel appropriate on a business card handed to a 55-year-old CEO
  • Suggest growth, progress, and upward trajectory
  • Have both horizontal and stacked versions
  • Include clear space guidelines
Logo Must Not
  • Use gradients or effects that don't reproduce in print
  • Feel like a tech startup or app company
  • Include cliche imagery (rockets, lightbulbs, handshakes)
  • Have so much detail it becomes illegible at small sizes
  • Look trendy or time-bound
  • Require color to be understood
  • Feel aggressive, disruptive, or confrontational

Section 10

Visual Guidelines Summary

Do This
  • Use generous white space - let the design breathe
  • Lead with benefits and outcomes in headlines
  • Include real numbers and metrics whenever possible
  • Show testimonials with real names and photos
  • Use gold strategically - it's the accent, not the base
  • Maintain consistent margins and padding
  • Use photography of actual business environments
  • Create clear visual hierarchy with typography
  • Test all designs at small sizes before finalizing
  • Include alt text and accessible color contrast
Don't Do This
  • Crowd layouts with too much information
  • Use trendy design effects (glassmorphism, neon gradients)
  • Include stock photos of generic "business people"
  • Use multiple accent colors in one piece
  • Make text smaller than 14px for body copy
  • Use animation or movement that distracts
  • Put important information in image text only
  • Use jargon or buzzwords without explanation
  • Create one-off "creative" executions that break guidelines
  • Prioritize style over substance
The Decision Filter
When evaluating any design decision, ask: "Would this build trust with a 55-year-old staffing agency owner who's been seeking certainty before?" If the answer isn't clearly yes, reconsider the choice. Every visual element should earn its place by supporting the core objective: immediate trust that leads to a conversation.

Every element works together to answer the fundamental question our personas are asking: "Can I trust these people with my business?"

The answer, through every visual touchpoint, must be an unequivocal yes.
