Section 01

Voice Essence

The ScaleUp Voice
The Trusted Advisor Who Proves Value Before Asking for Trust
ScaleUp Ventures speaks like a seasoned business consultant who has sat across the table from dozens of skeptical business owners. We know they have been burned by agencies that promised results and delivered excuses. So we do not ask for trust - we earn it by showing our work upfront. Our voice is confident because we are competent, direct because time is money, and warm because we genuinely care about the businesses we help. We speak in outcomes, not features. We quantify, not qualify. We say "here is the model" instead of "trust us."

Who We Are Speaking To

Our primary audience consists of technology laggard business owners - typically aged 50-65, running established businesses ($1M-$15M revenue), who built their success on relationships and industry expertise but now feel left behind by digital transformation. They are:

  • 1. Skeptical - They have been burned by agencies that overpromised and underdelivered
  • 2. Decisive - They did not build successful businesses by being wishy-washy
  • 3. Fearful of disruption - They see AI and digital competitors threatening their business model
  • 4. Value-conscious - They want ROI, not pretty deliverables
  • 5. Trust-driven - 91% are influenced by peer recommendations, 97% trust case studies
Section 02

Voice Attributes

1. Accountable
"We show you how you make your money back before you spend a dollar."
This Means
  • We quantify expected outcomes before projects begin
  • We tie our success to client success
  • We are specific about what we will deliver and when
  • We admit when something is not working
  • We use data and metrics, not vague promises
This Does NOT Mean
  • Making unrealistic guarantees
  • Promising outcomes we cannot control
  • Using lawyer-speak with excessive caveats
  • Hedging every statement until it means nothing
  • Blaming clients when things go wrong
Example In Practice
"Based on your current traffic and industry benchmarks, a redesigned website with our conversion optimization should generate 15-25 additional qualified leads per month. Here is the model - let me walk you through the assumptions so you can pressure-test them."
2. Confident
Expertise earned through experience, not claimed through credentials.
This Means
  • We make clear recommendations, not mealy-mouthed suggestions
  • We stand behind our work and our advice
  • We say "we recommend" not "you might consider"
  • We are comfortable saying "no" or "that will not work"
  • We lead with the answer, then explain the reasoning
This Does NOT Mean
  • Being arrogant or dismissive
  • Pretending we know everything
  • Talking down to clients
  • Refusing to acknowledge alternatives
  • Being inflexible when new information emerges
Example In Practice
"You need to fix your lead capture before running paid ads. Right now, you are paying to send traffic to a page that converts at 1.2%. That is burning money. Let us get your landing page to 3-4% first, then we will turn on the ads."
3. Direct
Respect their time. Say what you mean. Mean what you say.
This Means
  • We get to the point quickly
  • We use plain language, not jargon
  • We give the bottom line upfront
  • We are honest about timelines, costs, and challenges
  • We say the hard things when they need to be said
This Does NOT Mean
  • Being rude or curt
  • Skipping important context
  • Being blunt to the point of offense
  • Rushing clients who need to process information
  • Oversimplifying complex realities
Example In Practice
"This project will cost $18,000 and take 8 weeks. Here is what is included, here is what is not, and here is when you will see results. Any questions on those numbers?"
4. Grounded
Real results for real businesses. No hype, no fluff, no buzzwords.
This Means
  • We speak in concrete business terms (revenue, leads, cost savings)
  • We reference real examples and case studies
  • We acknowledge limitations and tradeoffs
  • We connect every recommendation to business outcomes
  • We are skeptical of trends and fads
This Does NOT Mean
  • Being pessimistic or cynical
  • Ignoring innovation or new approaches
  • Being boring or dry
  • Refusing to discuss aspirational outcomes
  • Downplaying genuine success stories
Example In Practice
"AI is not going to replace your recruiters. But it can cut the time they spend on resume screening by 60%, which means they can focus on what actually generates placements: building relationships with candidates and clients."
5. Partner-Minded
We succeed when you succeed. It is that simple.
This Means
  • We use "we" and "us" when discussing the work
  • We genuinely care about the client's business
  • We think long-term, not just about the current project
  • We recommend against work that will not help them
  • We celebrate client wins like our own
This Does NOT Mean
  • Being a pushover or yes-man
  • Avoiding difficult conversations
  • Pretending we are employees
  • Losing objectivity
  • Working for free or undervaluing our expertise
Example In Practice
"Honestly, I would not recommend the full website rebuild right now. Let us start with a landing page and run some ads. If we can prove the leads are there, then we can make a much stronger business case for the full investment."
Section 03

Voice Spectrum

Where ScaleUp Ventures sits on key communication dimensions. The gold marker shows our default position; actual tone may shift based on context.

Formal Casual
Professional but approachable. We skip the corporate stiffness but maintain credibility. Think "business meeting in a good restaurant" - not stuffy, not sloppy.
Serious Playful
We are dealing with people's livelihoods and business outcomes. We are serious about the work, though not humorless. Occasional wit is fine; forced jokes are not.
Technical Simple
We translate technical concepts into business impact. Our audience are business owners, not developers. Say "automated follow-up emails" not "drip campaign sequences with behavioral triggers."
Reserved Enthusiastic
Genuine enthusiasm for client wins and good work. But we are not cheerleaders - we are advisors. Energy should come from conviction, not from exclamation points.
Cautious Bold
We make clear recommendations and take positions. We are not afraid to say what we think. But bold does not mean reckless - our confidence is grounded in data and experience.
Section 04

Tone Variations by Context

While our voice stays consistent, our tone adapts to the context. Here is how the same ScaleUp voice sounds across different channels.

Website Copy
High Confidence Direct
Website copy is our first impression. Lead with the core differentiator (ROI-focused, pre-engagement modeling) and speak directly to the skeptical business owner. Every sentence should answer "why should I trust you?" Headlines are bold, body copy is specific, and CTAs are clear about the next step.
Do This
"We build a model showing how you make your money back before you spend a dollar. Then we execute - and we are accountable to the numbers we projected."
Not This
"We are a full-service digital agency committed to delivering excellence and driving growth for our valued partners through innovative solutions."
Email Communications
Warmer Efficient
Email is more personal. Open with context (why you are writing), get to the point quickly, and close with a clear next step. Use their name. Reference specific details from your relationship. Keep paragraphs short. Busy business owners scan - make it scannable.
Do This
"Mike - quick update on the landing page. We hit the 3.2% conversion target you needed, up from 1.4%. Here is what that means: at your current traffic, that is 12 more leads per month. Worth a quick call Thursday to discuss next steps?"
Not This
"Dear Valued Client, I hope this email finds you well. I wanted to take a moment to reach out regarding the recent work we have completed on your digital assets..."
Social Media (LinkedIn)
Conversational Thought Leadership
Social media is where we demonstrate expertise through insights, not sales pitches. Share perspectives on industry trends, celebrate client wins (with permission), and engage in discussions. More personality shows through here. Contrarian takes are fine when backed by logic.
Do This
"Hot take: most businesses should NOT redesign their website. They should fix their landing page and run ads first. Prove the leads exist before you build a $25K website. Here is why..."
Not This
"Excited to announce our new website redesign services! Contact us today for a free consultation! #DigitalMarketing #WebDevelopment #GrowthHacking"
Sales/Pitch Materials
Compelling Specific
Proposals and pitch decks need to do heavy lifting: establish credibility, demonstrate understanding of their situation, and make the path forward feel low-risk. Lead with their problem, show the ROI model early, be specific about deliverables and timeline. No fluff - every slide earns its place.
Do This
"Your current website converts at 1.2%. Industry benchmark is 3-4%. At your traffic level, that gap costs you 18 leads per month. This proposal closes that gap. Investment: $15,000. Projected ROI: 8x in year one. Here is how we calculated that."
Not This
"We propose a comprehensive digital transformation initiative leveraging best-in-class methodologies to optimize your customer acquisition funnel and drive sustainable growth."
Client Communications
Collaborative Transparent
Once they are a client, the relationship deepens. We are partners now, not vendors. Be more candid, share behind-the-scenes thinking, and do not hide challenges. Proactive communication builds trust. Celebrate wins together and tackle problems together.
Do This
"Heads up - we hit a snag with the CRM integration. The API documentation was wrong, so we are 3 days behind. Here is how we are fixing it, and here is the updated timeline. Still on track for the original launch date."
Not This
"Due to unforeseen technical complexities beyond our control, we regret to inform you that the project timeline has been adjusted. We apologize for any inconvenience this may cause."
Section 05

Do's and Don'ts

Do This
  • Lead with the outcome or bottom line, then explain the reasoning
  • Use specific numbers and metrics whenever possible
  • Acknowledge objections and address them directly
  • Reference real examples, case studies, and data points
  • Use "we" when discussing the work (partner mentality)
  • Make clear recommendations - take a position
  • Translate technical concepts into business impact
  • Be honest about limitations, timelines, and costs
  • Celebrate client wins and give them the credit
  • Use active voice and action verbs
  • Keep sentences and paragraphs short
  • End with clear next steps or calls to action
Avoid This
  • Using vague buzzwords: "synergy," "leverage," "holistic," "seamless"
  • Making promises without backing them up with data
  • Hiding behind passive voice or corporate speak
  • Overselling or using exclamation points excessively
  • Being condescending about their current technology
  • Using fear-mongering to create urgency
  • Padding proposals with unnecessary content
  • Speaking in features instead of outcomes
  • Using jargon that makes us sound smart but confuses clients
  • Making everything about us instead of them
  • Hedging so much that we say nothing
  • Using trendy phrases: "crushing it," "game-changer," "disrupt"
Section 06

Before/After Examples

Example 1
Homepage Hero Copy
Before (Off-Brand)
"We are a leading digital consultancy delivering innovative solutions that empower businesses to achieve their full potential through strategic technology implementation and data-driven insights."
After (On-Brand)
"We build a model showing how you make your money back before you spend a dollar. Then we execute - and we are accountable to the numbers we projected."
Why it works: Leads with the core differentiator (ROI modeling), uses plain language, makes a specific claim that can be verified, and positions us as accountable partners.
Example 2
Proposal Introduction
Before (Off-Brand)
"Thank you for the opportunity to present this proposal. At ScaleUp Ventures, we pride ourselves on delivering best-in-class solutions tailored to meet your unique business needs. We look forward to partnering with you on this exciting journey."
After (On-Brand)
"You told us you need 20 more leads per month. This proposal shows how we get you there. Investment: $18,000. Timeline: 8 weeks. Projected ROI: 6x in year one. Here is the math and the plan."
Why it works: Skips the pleasantries and gets straight to what matters - their goal, our solution, the numbers. Shows we listened and can quantify our impact.
Example 3
Service Description
Before (Off-Brand)
"Our comprehensive AI automation solutions leverage cutting-edge machine learning technologies to streamline your operational workflows and enhance productivity across your organization."
After (On-Brand)
"We set up systems that handle the repetitive work your team hates - following up with leads, sending appointment reminders, routing service calls. Your people focus on what actually makes money."
Why it works: Translates technical capability into daily reality. Uses concrete examples they can picture. Connects to business outcome (making money).
Example 4
Addressing Concerns About AI
Before (Off-Brand)
"Embrace the AI revolution! Our transformative AI solutions will catapult your business into the future and ensure you do not get left behind in this rapidly evolving landscape."
After (On-Brand)
"AI is not going to replace your business. But competitors using AI to work faster and cheaper might. We will show you exactly where AI can save you time and money - no hype, just practical applications that pay for themselves."
Why it works: Acknowledges their real fear (being replaced), reframes it accurately (competitors), promises practical value, and sets expectation of proof.
Example 5
Project Update (Bad News)
Before (Off-Brand)
"Due to unforeseen circumstances and complexities that arose during the implementation phase, we regret to inform you that there may be a potential delay in the projected timeline. We are working diligently to minimize any impact."
After (On-Brand)
"We are three days behind schedule. The CRM integration took longer than expected because their API documentation was outdated. Here is how we are making up the time, and here is the updated timeline. We are still hitting the launch date."
Why it works: Leads with the news, explains the specific cause, shows the solution, and gives confidence about the outcome. No hedging, no blame-shifting.

Section 08

Phrase Guide

Say This, Not That
Instead of This Say This Why
"We are an ROI-focused agency" "We show you the ROI model before you invest" Everyone claims ROI-focused. The mechanism is the differentiator.
"We deliver results" "Here is what we delivered for a similar client: [specific metrics]" Specific is believable. Vague is ignorable.
"We partner with you" "We succeed when you succeed" Shows aligned incentives, not just relationship language.
"Trusted by hundreds of businesses" "Talk to three of our clients before you decide" Demonstrates confidence. Puts proof where the claim is.
"We offer comprehensive solutions" "We handle development, marketing, and automation" Specific services are memorable. "Comprehensive" is not.
"Our team of experts" "Our developers have built [X] sites in your industry" Specificity builds credibility. Claims need evidence.
"We would love to learn more about your needs" "Tell me about the leads you need and I will show you how we can get there" Signals expertise and moves toward outcomes immediately.
"Let us schedule a call to discuss" "Can we do 20 minutes Thursday? I will show you the math." Specific time, specific agenda. Respects their time.
"We are committed to excellence" "We build to our standards or we rebuild until we get it right" Concrete commitment versus empty promise.
"At the end of the day" "The bottom line is..." Less cliched, more direct.

Voice Check: Before You Publish

Ask yourself these questions before publishing any content:

  1. Is there a specific number or metric I can include? If not, can I add one?
  2. Would a skeptical business owner believe this? Or does it sound like every other agency?
  3. Am I leading with their problem or our capabilities? Lead with them.
  4. Could I cut 25% of this and lose nothing? Then cut it.
  5. Is my recommendation clear? Or am I hedging too much?
  6. Would I say this to a client in person? If not, rewrite it.
  7. Is there a clear next step? Every piece of content should lead somewhere.

Remember

Our audience has been burned by agencies that overpromised and underdelivered. They are smart, successful people who built real businesses. They do not need to be impressed - they need to be convinced. Every word we write should earn their trust by being specific, honest, and valuable. When in doubt, show our work.
